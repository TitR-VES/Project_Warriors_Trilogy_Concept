## 1. CORE PREMISE

**Project Warriors: The Storm** is a clandestine Far East resistance campaign centered on **Procella** and the Pacific edge of a collapsing Russian state. The campaign is not about large-scale naval warfare, not about conventional front-line liberation, and not about a glossy espionage fantasy. It is about the mechanics of survival and resistance at the edge: routes, weather, batteries, relays, boats, false data, frightened civilians, and the ugly practical work of getting people and information out alive.

The campaign follows **Denis “Sakhalin” Malinin**, a Russian Far East littoral field operator whose background combines reconnaissance-grade maritime field competence with years spent inside the neglected technical skeleton of the region. He is not a speechmaker or symbol. He is the man who can keep a shoreline extraction route functional for ten more minutes when ten more minutes is the difference between survival and disappearance.

This DLC should read as:
- coastal
- cold
- practical
- human
- deniable
- systems-aware
- morally pressurized
- and distinctly regional

# PROJECT WARRIORS — SCREENPLAY BEAT v0.1  
## MISSION 01 — SOUTH CULVERT  
### Opening Approach

**Status:** working screenplay beat  
**Campaign:** *Project Warriors: The Storm*  
**Playable character:** Denis Nikolaevich Malinin / SAKHALIN  
**Sequence function:** tone-setter, character introduction through competence, route-reading grammar, first campaign atmosphere lock

**EXT. COASTAL SERVICE ROAD — DUSK**

A dead stretch of cracked service road above black water.

Not open shoreline.  
Not clean industrial space.  
Something between the two.

A wire fence sags toward a drainage cut choked with old reeds and blown trash. Beyond it, the sea is only a darker band inside the dark. Far off, a scatter of harbor lights trembles in mist. One sodium lamp on a leaning pole burns weak and wrong, buzzing in the wind.

Wet asphalt. Standing water in old wheel ruts. Rust on everything that once had corners.

A FIGURE moves up the road without hurry.

DENIS NIKOLAEVICH MALININ, 39. Lean, layered dark weather gear, compact rifle slung close, gloves, beanie, rough stubble, face shaped by cold and bad sleep. Nothing theatrical in him. He walks like a man already sorting the place into useful and not useful.

The wind pushes at him. He does not react to it.

He reaches a concrete barrier half-swallowed by weeds and stops.

Listens.

Not dramatic listening. Calibrated. Working.

Water below. Cable hum somewhere inland. Sheet metal tapping out of rhythm. Then—

A metallic rattle from the culvert line ahead.

Denis looks toward it. Waits.

Nothing follows.

He moves again.

---

**EXT. FENCE GAP / DRAINAGE EDGE — CONTINUOUS**

The road gives way to a narrow descent along a chain-link fence that has been cut once, repaired badly, then cut again more carefully.

Denis crouches by the opening.

Touches the wire.

Fresh scrape on galvanized metal. Not much. Enough.

He looks at the mud below the fence line.

One partial bootprint, half-filled with water.

Not old.

He glances toward the sea, then toward the service corridor inland.

A beat.

He slips through the fence.

---

**EXT. DRAINAGE CUT — CONTINUOUS**

Concrete walls stained black with years of runoff. Water moving shallow and fast through the center trench. Pipe mouths under the road. Broken maintenance steps slick with algae.

Denis moves along the edge, boots finding the dry line without looking like he’s looking.

He passes a dead utility box with its door hanging open.

Stops.

Inside: old junctions, stripped wiring, one newer clamp with clean tool marks.

That does not belong.

He studies it for a second too long for it to be casual, then closes the door without forcing it.

Keeps moving.

A RADIO EARPIECE clicks softly.

Not chatter. A recorded fragment. Short-range dead-drop style.

A woman’s voice, low and flat, not trying to sound mysterious.

**VOICE (COMMS)**  
South culvert. Then wait.

Static.

Denis says nothing.

He touches the earpiece once, kills the replay, and keeps moving.

---

**EXT. CULVERT APPROACH CHANNEL — MOMENTS LATER**

The drainage cut opens toward a broader concrete throat running under the rail embankment. Water noise deepens. Wind falls off. The place feels suddenly enclosed, sound trapped and redirected.

A faded maintenance stencil on the wall has been painted over, then weathered through again.

Someone has tied a strip of dark electrical tape around a rusted bracket ahead.

A marker.

Denis sees it from twenty meters out.

He slows.

Looks left wall. Right wall. Ceiling seam. Waterline. Gravel shelf. Pipe mouths.

The shelf under the bracket is empty.

Whatever was supposed to be there is gone.

Denis kneels.

On the concrete: a cigarette butt swollen with damp. Recent.

He does not touch it.

Nearby, a tiny crescent of flaked paint, fresh against the older grime.

He follows the line with his eyes.

Bullet graze.

Not random.

He looks deeper into the culvert.

No body. No bag. No waiting contact.

Just darkness and runoff.

Then he spots it—

A small maintenance hatch on the right wall. Not fully shut.

He rises and approaches at an angle.

Rifle comes forward, compact, unshowy.

He opens the hatch with his left hand.

Inside: not much space. Cables, old valves, pooled water, grime.

And tucked behind a conduit clamp—

A cheap waterproof pouch.

Denis takes it out.

Inside: a battery the size of a matchbox, wrapped in black tape, and a folded scrap of paper sealed in plastic.

He opens the note.

Three words.

**LAMP WRONG. DON’T WAIT.**

Denis reads it once.

Looks up toward the weak sodium glow bleeding into the culvert mouth behind him.

The lamp buzzes again. Too steady. Too visible.

Not failed. Forced on.

He folds the note. Pockets it.

A faint engine note carries in from inland. Very far. Or not far enough.

Denis keys his mic once.

**DENIS**  
Route’s damaged. Not dead.

A beat. No answer yet.

He studies the culvert floor again.

There it is now — once he knows where to look.

A smear where someone was dragged, but only for a meter or two. After that, nothing.

Not a clean snatch.

A struggle interrupted.

A choice made quickly.

Denis’s eyes shift to the water channel, then to a narrow secondary runoff pipe half-hidden by shadow and reeds.

Too small for comfort. Big enough for a desperate adult.

He understands the shape of it.

Not all of it. Enough.

Another click in the earpiece. Different voice. Male. Older. Calm.

Compressed to near nothing by bad transmission.

**MALE VOICE (COMMS)**  
If usable, mark cold. Not warm.

Static.

Denis freezes for half a second.

Not at the instruction.

At the fact that whoever sent it already knows the route may still be alive.

He looks at the dead lamp again. Then at the hidden runoff pipe.

He answers quietly.

**DENIS**  
Copy.

He pulls a stub of blue survey chalk from a pouch, reaches low behind the bracket where casual eyes would miss it, and draws a short horizontal line under the rust flange.

Cold.

No theatrics. No symbol.

Just work.

The engine sound comes again. Clearer now.

Denis steps back into shadow, rifle tight in, eyes on the culvert mouth, the hidden pipe, the glow of the false lamp, all of it at once.

The route is not clean.

The route still exists.

For the moment.

**CUT TO BLACK.**

**EXT. LOWER SERVICE CHANNEL / WATERLINE SEAM — CONTINUOUS**

Concrete. Rusted ladder feet. Tide noise under steel.

Denis moves through the dark by memory and shape more than sight. Above him, tires roll slowly over wet pavement. Doors slam. Muffled voices. Japanese, fast and irritated. Another voice behind it, sharper, procedural.

They are early for a coincidence. Late for a trap.

About right for this city.

Denis reaches a drainage recess half-hidden by hanging conduit and crouches inside it.

Waits.

A flashlight beam cuts across the platform above, white and hard. Misses the lower seam entirely.

One of the voices calls out. Another answers from farther off.

The beam passes.

A beat.

Older voice. Calm. Compressed by distance and bad transmission.

Still breathing?

For now.

Static hum.

Real enough.

I know.
