OVERVIEW & WORKING METHOD
--------------------------
This file documents the full teaser video treatments for all three
Game 2 campaigns developed in the promotional sidebar thread, along
with the creative rationale, collaborative decisions, and principles
that emerged from the development process. It is intended to be
uploaded to the project knowledge base so that future threads can
benefit from what was established here.

The three teasers were developed sequentially — Romeo first, then
Ducky, then Spook — using a consistent method: Claude proposed a
full concept, Stefan responded with additions and modifications,
and each treatment was refined through that exchange before being
written up as a clean final document. All final creative calls
were made by Stefan.

CAMPAIGN-BY-CAMPAIGN EMOTIONAL REGISTER SUMMARY
-------------------------------------------------
AMERICAN FREEDOM (Romeo):
  Elegiac, documentary, grief before defiance. The teaser shows
  what has been lost before it shows any resistance. Emotional
  architecture: grief → weight → quiet defiance → reckoning.

AEGIS FALLS (Ducky):
  Dread and institutional fracture. The feeling of standing on a
  fault line the moment before it gives. Emotional architecture:
  trust → creeping wrongness → operational chaos → the promise
  broken. The returning AEGIS emblem (pristine → cracked →
  fractured) is the visual spine.

GODKILLER (Spook):
  Psychological menace and the gap between official truth and
  actual truth. Investigative rather than action-driven. Emotional
  architecture: institutional certainty → evidence of deception →
  the hunt → the confrontation of two men who understand each other
  completely.


KEY CREATIVE DECISIONS MADE IN THIS THREAD
--------------------------------------------
1. CHORAL ARRANGEMENT — AMERICAN FREEDOM CLOSE
   Stefan's suggestion: the Good Morning America melody transitions
   in the final act not to a music swell but to a sparse unaccompanied
   mixed choir arrangement. Tonal reference: "Keep Your Rifle By Your
   Side" from the Ken Burns Civil War soundtrack — voices that sound
   like they belong to the land, somewhere between a hymn and a lament.
   The effect: Romeo at the tree line stops being one soldier and
   becomes something larger. The melody now belongs to everyone in
   the town. Hard cut to black stops the choir mid-phrase — the
   silence lands harder for what it interrupted.

2. THREE-FRAME CODY BEAUMONT IMAGE — AMERICAN FREEDOM BUILD SECTION
   Stefan's suggestion: buried in the quick cuts of Act Three, lasting
   approximately three frames (roughly one eighth of a second at 24fps),
   is a flash of the Cody Beaumont death image — the ambiguous loading
   screen photograph that is one of the campaign's most emotionally
   loaded visual assets. It is not acknowledged by the edit around it.
   It is gone before the conscious mind fully forms the question.
   Placement confirmed: inside the momentum of the build section cuts,
   not near the emotional close. Purpose: plants subconscious unease,
   rewards frame-by-frame analysis, respects the intelligence of the
   audience, generates organic discovery/discussion post-release.
   Both Stefan and Claude independently arrived at the same placement
   instinct — confirmed as correct.

3. RAW POV/DRONE FOOTAGE GRAMMAR — AEGIS FALLS
   Stefan's suggestion: some of the quick cuts in Ducky's teaser
   should reference the visual grammar of modern warfare as audiences
   now know it from Ukrainian war footage online — GoPro helmet
   footage, drone POV, night vision green, thermal white-hot
   signatures, handheld phone video with blown-out audio. The
   frequency of this raw footage material should ramp up and down
   in sync with the music and pacing — rare during the measured
   opening acts, bleeding in with increasing frequency during the
   build, peaking near the climax, then receding as the music
   hollows out toward the close. First act kept entirely cinematic
   so the raw footage's first appearance carries weight as an
   intrusion. Claude confirmed this is not a step in the wrong
   direction — it has real cinematic and editorial precedent and
   the ramp logic is sophisticated and correct.

4. TWO LINES OF DIALOGUE — GODKILLER CLOSE
   The only spoken words across all three teasers appear at the
   close of Godkiller:
     SPOOK: "You can't kill a dead man."
     GOLDBERG (O.S.): "No."
   Rationale: the tagline IS dialogue, and hearing it spoken lands
   differently than reading it. Goldberg's single-word response —
   calm, faintly amused — is more unsettling than any image.
   It establishes his presence without showing his face clearly.
   Stefan confirmed: keep the dialogue. Do not apply the no-dialogue
   rule to Godkiller.

5. RETURNING EMBLEM STRUCTURE — AEGIS FALLS
   The AEGIS emblem appears three times across the teaser —
   Act One (pristine), Act Two close (hairline crack, easy to miss),
   Act Four (spider web fracture, still holding its shape, still
   recognizable, motto still legible). The visual argument made
   without words: the system you trusted was already cracking.
   You just couldn't see it yet.

6. EAST MCFARLANE ISLAND — GODKILLER
   Appears in Act Three as the cut that sits longer than everything
   around it — wide aerial, golden hour, Goldberg alive and unhurried
   on a terrace overlooking the water. His posture is that of a man
   who has decided he is entirely beyond reach. This image is load-
   bearing: it confirms to the audience that the DECEASED stamp was
   wrong, without a single word of explanation.

TEASER ONE: AMERICAN FREEDOM

AMERICAN FREEDOM
Campaign Teaser — Full Treatment
Music: "Good Morning America" — Jesse Welles

FORMAT
Runtime: approx. 90 seconds
No dialogue. No HUD. No gameplay footage.
Aspect ratio: 2.39:1 cinematic widescreen
Grade: desaturated, cool morning light, high contrast shadows

---

ACT ONE — RECOGNITION
0:00 — 0:20

Black screen. Silence. Two full beats.

The first guitar notes of Good Morning America come in barely
audible — like a radio signal finding its frequency from a long
way away.

Image fades in slowly. Pre-dawn blue light. The camera drifts
forward at roof height, unhurried, almost floating, down what is
unmistakably a Main Street in small-town America. Storefronts.
A diner with its lights still off. A church steeple against a
pale sky. Fall foliage. It looks almost peaceful.

The camera drifts lower.

A Russian military checkpoint fills the intersection ahead.
A BTR parked across two lanes. Soldiers in unfamiliar uniforms,
breath visible in the cold morning air. A Russian flag hanging
from a telephone pole, completely still.

No dramatic sting. No music swell. Just that image, held long
enough to land.

---

ACT TWO — WEIGHT
0:20 — 0:50

The camera keeps moving — slow, documentary, without commentary.
We see the town in fragments.

A shuttered hardware store, plywood boards spray-painted with a
message in English. A burned-out house, two or three survivors
and neighbors quietly picking through the wreckage in the early
light, shovels moving slowly. A child's face at a curtained
window watching a patrol pass below. A handwritten sign in a
shop window in English and Russian. An elderly man sitting on
his porch steps, hands folded, watching nothing in particular.

Jesse Welles' voice carries the melody. The music still sounds
like home. The images don't look like home anymore. The contrast
does the emotional work without comment.

One image held a beat longer than the others: a partially burned
American flag still hanging from a porch railing, moving almost
imperceptibly in the morning cold.

No comment. Just that.

---

ACT THREE — CURRENT
0:50 — 1:10

The music begins to shift — not into anything loud, just a subtle
change in emotional register underneath the melody, like something
waking up.

Quick cuts. Still no dialogue.

A hand pressing a magazine into a rifle in near-darkness.
Mud-caked boots moving along a treeline. A face — Romeo's,
half-lit by weak morning light — watching a patrol pass from
cover, completely still. A resistance contact sliding a folded
paper across a table without making eye contact. A flare climbing
into a grey sky somewhere in the middle distance. Shadowed figures
moving with quiet urgency through the back of a barn.

Buried here, in the momentum of the cuts, lasting approximately
three frames:

THE CODY BEAUMONT IMAGE.

Not dwelt upon. Not explained. Gone before the conscious mind can
fully form the question. Something is wrong with what you just saw.
You're not sure what.

The cuts continue. Nothing acknowledges it.

Romeo again — moving now, low and deliberate through undergrowth,
toward the edge of a tree line.

---

ACT FOUR — RECKONING
1:10 — 1:28

The quick cuts stop.

One wide, still shot: Romeo at the top of a hill, standing at the
edge of the tree line. His back is to the camera. He is looking
down at the town below — the checkpoints, the unfamiliar flags,
the smoke from a chimney here and there, ordinary life ground down
to its most essential and most fragile.

He doesn't move.

The guitar melody of Good Morning America begins to fall away —
and in its place, quietly at first and then with full weight, the
same melody rises again in unaccompanied human voices. A mixed
choir, sparse and unhurried, somewhere between a hymn and a
lament. The tonal reference is the Civil War vocal tradition —
voices that sound like they belong to the land, not to a recording
studio. The melody has grown larger than one man with a guitar.
It belongs to everyone in that town now.

Romeo still doesn't move.

---

CLOSE
1:28 — 1:35

Hard cut to black.

The choir stops mid-phrase.

Silence. One full beat.

---

White text. Plain. No fanfare. No musical swell beneath it.

WAR HITS HOME.

One beat.

AMERICAN FREEDOM.

One beat.

Coming Q2 2028.

Fade.

TEASER TWO: AEGIS FALLS
----------------------------------------------------------------

AEGIS FALLS
Campaign Teaser — Full Treatment
Music: "Kyiv" — Apashe ft. Alina Pash

FORMAT
Runtime: approx. 90 seconds
No dialogue. No HUD. No gameplay footage.
Aspect ratio: 2.39:1 cinematic widescreen
Grade: cold, high contrast, deep shadows — steel blue and amber
where light exists
Visual grammar: two modes — cinematic widescreen and raw
POV/drone footage. POV material is ungraded, aspect ratio native
to source, audio authentic. The two grammars are kept visually
distinct at all times.

---

ACT ONE — THE PROMISE
0:00 — 0:15

Black. Then Alina Pash's voice comes in alone — just the vocal,
unaccompanied, in Ukrainian. No orchestration yet. The language
sounds simultaneously foreign and deeply familiar to Western ears.
Beautiful and melancholy and completely unafraid.

A single image fades in slowly against it.

The AEGIS emblem. Clean and sharp, rendered in the established
briefing screen aesthetic — dark background, gold and silver
insignia, the Latin motto beneath it.

Recte Faciendo Neminem Timeas.

It looks authoritative. Trustworthy. Like something that has never
failed and has no reason to expect that it will.

Hold on it. Let Alina Pash's voice carry it.

Then cut to black.

---

ACT TWO — THE WEIGHT
0:15 — 0:50

The orchestration begins to build underneath the vocal, slowly,
with intention.

All footage in this section is cinematic. No POV material yet.

Geography established through feeling rather than labels. The flat
grey expanse of the North Sea at night, surface broken by wind.
The massive industrial silhouette of Rotterdam's port infrastructure
against a low sky. The angular geometry of a communications relay
station surrounded by deep Arctic snow — we don't name it Svalbard,
but the cold names it for us. Wet cobblestones in a European city
at night, boots moving with controlled urgency.

Then the operational texture. AEGIS in environments that should be
controlled but aren't quite behaving that way. A door that opens
onto something unexpected, a beat of stillness before the operator
moves. A contact who isn't where they're supposed to be. A screen
showing data that contradicts the brief. Frost at a tall window,
mask on, completely still, watching something below that the camera
does not show us. Ducky on a radio, expression unreadable,
listening to something we cannot hear.

Woven between these — brief, almost incidental — the details that
are wrong underneath the surface. A name visible on a document for
just long enough to register. A timestamp that doesn't add up if
you're paying attention. A familiar face in a place it has no
business being.

Nothing explained. Everything felt.

The music continues to build. Alina Pash's voice rising, the
orchestration thickening and broadening around it.

We return to the AEGIS emblem.

The same image. But there is a crack running through it now —
hairline thin, barely visible, easy to dismiss as a trick of the
light. The motto still perfectly legible beneath it.

Cut away before the viewer has fully decided what they saw.

---

ACT THREE — THE FRACTURE
0:50 — 1:10

The music hits its first real peak. The teaser shifts register.

This is where the raw POV material enters for the first time —
and its arrival is felt precisely because the first two acts earned
it. A burst of helmet-mounted GoPro footage, ungraded, slightly
blown out, the audio raw and immediate. Combat in a European
industrial environment, close and fast and without the composed
geometry of cinematic framing. Then back to cinematic. Then raw
again, slightly longer this time. Then cinematic. The alternation
accelerates in sync with the music — the two grammars trading
places with increasing frequency as the build intensifies, until
near the peak they are cutting against each other almost measure
for measure.

The raw footage carries that specific visual grammar of modern
warfare as people actually know it now. Drone footage, the eerie
detached silence of it before impact. Night vision green. Thermal
white-hot signatures of vehicles moving through darkness. A
handheld shot of a street engagement, audio blown out by nearby
concussion. The intimacy and inhumanity existing in the same frame
simultaneously.

Interspersed with both grammars — the operational reality of what
is going wrong. AEGIS in cover in an environment that was supposed
to be friendly. Ducky making a decision we don't understand with
information we don't have. Frost firing from a position that tells
us the plan has already failed. Switchblade pulling someone out of
a collapsing space, not looking up, focused entirely on the person
in her hands.

The question the campaign is actually asking moves underneath all
of it, never surfaced directly, only accumulated:

Who built this. Who funded it. Who does it actually serve.

The music reaches its peak — and then hollows out. The
orchestration falls away beneath Alina Pash's voice, leaving her
exposed and solitary and completely clear.

The raw POV footage recedes with the orchestration. The last few
cuts are cinematic again — stilled, composed. The visual grammar
settling back into itself as the music does.

---

ACT FOUR — THE RECKONING
1:10 — 1:28

One final image, held steady against that lone vocal.

The AEGIS emblem. Third and final time.

It is fractured now. Not destroyed — fractured. The crack from
before has spread across the face of the shield in a spider web
pattern, the way safety glass breaks — still holding its shape,
still recognizable as what it was, the structure intact but
fundamentally and irreversibly compromised. Every line of the
insignia still visible. The motto still legible beneath it.

Recte Faciendo Neminem Timeas.

Hold on it. Let Alina Pash carry the silence around it.

Then cut to black. The vocal stops.

Silence. One full beat.

---

White text. Plain. No fanfare.

THE SYSTEM FELL.

One beat.

WE DIDN'T.

One beat.

AEGIS FALLS.

One beat.

Coming Q2 2028.

Fade.

TEASER THREE: GODKILLER
----------------------------------------------------------------

GODKILLER
Campaign Teaser — Full Treatment
Music: "King Nothing" — Metallica

FORMAT
Runtime: approx. 90 seconds
Minimal dialogue — two lines at close only.
No HUD. No gameplay footage.
Aspect ratio: 2.39:1 cinematic widescreen
Grade: high contrast, deep shadows, warm amber and cold white
where artificial light exists — the palette of late nights in
rooms where important things are decided and never recorded

---

ACT ONE — THE FILE
0:00 — 0:20

Black. Silence.

King Nothing begins — not the opening riff, but the single breath
of anticipation just before it. One beat of quiet that makes the
guitar's arrival inevitable.

Then the riff hits and we open on:

A pair of hands — Spook's — laying a photograph face up on a
table in a dark room. We don't see his face yet. Just the hands,
and the photograph, and a pool of artificial light falling across
a surface covered in documents, strings, photographs, case files.
The architecture of an investigation that has been running for a
long time and has not yet found its floor.

The photograph is of Goldberg. Composed. Confident. The face of
a man who has never been held accountable for anything and has no
expectation that this will change.

Below the photograph, stamped in red:

DECEASED.

Spook's hand rests on the photograph for a moment. Still. Then he
slides it to one side and keeps working.

He doesn't believe it either.

---

ACT TWO — THE TRAIL
0:20 — 0:55

King Nothing drives this section. The music has momentum and the
teaser uses it.

We move through the world of the campaign in quick, kinetic cuts —
but always refracted through the specific lens of intelligence
work rather than conventional action. This is not Romeo's war.
This is not Ducky's institutional crisis. This is the world as
seen through surveillance, through intercepted documents, through
the gap between what the official record says and what is actually
true underneath it.

Fragments. Locations felt rather than named or labeled. Rain-
slicked streets of a European city at night, headlights smearing
across wet stone. The interior of a safehouse lived in for too
long — takeout containers, multiple laptops, a corkboard dense
with material. An airport concourse, Spook and his team moving
through the civilian flow like ghosts, unhurried, invisible.
A skyscraper face at night, a figure rappelling down glass toward
a lit window below. A tropical compound perimeter at night,
security lights cutting hard lines through the dark.

Interspersed with the locations — the paper trail. Fragments of
documents, never held long enough to fully read. Photographs with
names partially visible. Redacted lines that confirm something is
present without revealing what. A name appearing across multiple
separate files in separate contexts, connecting things that should
have no connection.

And woven through all of it — faces. Goldberg. Stockbridge.
Macmillan. Crodyn. Each appearing briefly, never labeled, never
explained. Simply present. Watching from inside their photographs
and their files with the particular composure of people who have
never had reason to be afraid of being seen.

Then a cut that lands differently than the ones around it:

A television screen. News broadcast, slightly grainy. A chyron
running beneath footage of a federal prison facility. We cannot
read it fully — only fragments. Explosion. Federal facility.
One confirmed dead.

Then the photograph of Goldberg again. Different photograph.
Same stamp.

DECEASED.

Spook's face for the first time — lit from below by the cold
light of a screen, expression unreadable, somewhere between fury
and something that might almost be recognition. The look of a man
who has just been told a joke he already knew the punchline to
and is not remotely amused by it.

---

ACT THREE — THE HUNT
0:55 — 1:15

The music moves into its harder register and the teaser opens up.

Brief, controlled glimpses of action — not sustained sequences,
just enough to establish what is at stake and what these men are
willing to do to reach it. A close quarters engagement in a dark
interior, fast and without ceremony. Spook moving through a
burning space with the unhurried focus of a man looking for
something specific amid the chaos. Tripper at a keyboard inside
a vehicle moving fast through a city at night. Smoke back against
a wall beside a doorway, covering the approach, waiting.

But the action is never the point here. Every action beat is
interrupted by or resolves into another intelligence fragment —
another piece of the picture assembling itself into something
larger and more dangerous than any single mission.

Until one cut that arrives without announcement and sits longer
than everything around it:

East McFarlane Island. Wide aerial shot, golden hour light, the
water around it the deep blue of somewhere that does not appear
on maps that ordinary people consult. Isolated. Beautiful.
Completely self-contained.

Then Goldberg — alive. Standing at the edge of a terrace
overlooking the water, back partially to camera, unhurried,
looking at the horizon with the posture of a man who has decided
he is entirely beyond reach. No urgency. No fear. Just a man
standing on his island in the golden hour, watching the water.

Then black. Sudden and total.

---

ACT FOUR — THE EXCHANGE
1:15 — 1:28

The music strips back to almost nothing. Just the guitar, quiet.

Spook's voice — low, flat, certain. The only spoken words in
the teaser:

"You can't kill a dead man."

A beat of silence.

Then another voice — older, quieter, somewhere in the register
of a man who finds most things faintly amusing and has learned
not to show it too obviously:

"No."

Cut to black. Music stops completely.

Silence. One full beat.

---

White text. Plain. No fanfare. No music beneath it.

YOU CAN'T KILL A DEAD MAN.

One beat.

GODKILLER.

One beat.

Coming Q2 2028.

Fade.
