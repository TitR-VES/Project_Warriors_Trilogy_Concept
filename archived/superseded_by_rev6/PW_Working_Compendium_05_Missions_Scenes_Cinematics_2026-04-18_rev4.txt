SCENE 1 — THE GRIPEN FLYOVER [FULL TREATMENT]
Aircraft: JAS 39 Gripen (x2) — PHAETON Flight, Swedish Air Force
DAOG Designation: PHAETON
Campaign: Persistent Threat (Ducky) — Game 2
Placement: TBD — tentatively Hard Winter or Dead Frequency

--------

CONCEPT

A world-scale moment embedded in a ground-level mission. The player — as Ducky — exits dense forest or terrain cover and enters a clearing with open sky. Swedish Air Force Gripens pass in a low fast attack run, deploying into a break maneuver after the pass. The scene establishes that AEGIS is operating inside something much larger than a four-person squad, and briefly makes the squad feel small inside something vast — before contracting back to ground level. Frost's single line afterward is the scene's emotional punctuation.

AUDIO ARCHITECTURE — THREE PHASES

Phase One — The Frequency: Radio chatter begins as ambient background — not directed at the player, not requiring response. In Swedish. The player who doesn't speak Swedish hears cadence and register — professional, clipped, military aviation communication rhythm — without understanding content. This is intentional. A brief exchange in English on DAOG liaison frequency establishes position and intent:

"AEGIS element, this is PHAETON flight, two Gripens, we have you visual. Ten clicks northeast, maintaining altitude. Standing by for tasking."

Ducky responds briefly — confirms position, acknowledges support. Radio returns to Swedish between the two aircraft.

Phase Two — The Approach: Squad continues moving. Forest thins. Sound design begins its work — jet noise building from a hum that could be wind, to a frequency the player's body registers before their ears consciously identify it, to something unmistakably mechanical and fast and getting faster. Directionality shifts — behind, above, closing. The clearing opens. The sky appears. Player has full control throughout.

Phase Three — The Exposure: Gripens come in low and fast from behind the player's position. Sound arrives before the aircraft — engine noise reaches full intensity in the half-second before jets cross the player's field of view. They are overhead and gone in under three seconds. The player who was looking at the right part of the sky catches them fully. The player who was looking elsewhere catches the blur, the shadow, the physical pressure of the sound.

CAMERA NOTE

A dynamic camera pull mimics the involuntary response of a trained person to fast-moving aircraft overhead. Not taking control away from the player — nudging the camera upward with just enough force that looking up feels like something their character did rather than something the game did to them. If it feels like the game is making them look, it breaks immersion. If it feels like Ducky's head just moved, it deepens it.

POST-PASS

Gripens peel off after the pass — one banking left, one banking right. The specific visual of a two-ship breaking into separate egress vectors. Afterburner catch in the light if the time of day supports it. Then gone, climbing, sound receding as fast as it arrived.

"AEGIS, PHAETON flight departing for tasking. Good hunting."

Then Swedish again between the two pilots — briefly, something that sounds like it might be humor. The specific register of two people in high-performance aircraft who are very good at what they do and know it. Then silence. The forest. The squad. The scale contracting back to four people on the ground.

FROST'S LINE

After a moment:

"Finnish Air Force flies Hornets."

Nobody asks why he said that. Nobody needs to.

THEMATIC FUNCTION

The scene expands the player's perceptual world from squad-sized to theater-sized, then contracts it back. The contraction is as important as the expansion — the contrast produces the feeling. The player feels small after the Gripens peel off. But small in the specific way of someone who is part of something large, not the small of someone who is alone. The radio chatter in Swedish communicates that the world does not exist to serve the player's comprehension of it. Allied forces speak to each other in their own languages. The war has its own life outside the missions.

SCENE 2 — THE A400M PASSAGE [FULL TREATMENT]
Aircraft: Airbus A400M Atlas — ICARUS Flight, Belgian Air Component / DAOG
DAOG Designation: ICARUS
Campaign: Persistent Threat (Ducky) — Game 2
Placement: TBD — tentatively Permafrost (Svalbard approach) or Cold Open withdrawal

--------

CONCEPT

Where the Gripen scene is fast, kinetic, gone before the player fully processes it — the A400M passage is slow. Enormous. Impossible to ignore and impossible to hurry. The A400M is one of the largest military transport aircraft currently in service. Its four turboprop engines produce a sound completely unlike jet noise — lower, more pervasive, with a physical quality that fighter jets do not have. You hear an A400M coming for a long time before you see it, and continue to hear it for a long time after it is gone. This is the scene's entire mechanism. Length. Duration. The aircraft taking an unreasonable amount of time to cross the sky, and the player unable to look away.

SETUP — THE ANTICIPATED ARRIVAL

Unlike the Gripen scene — which catches the player mid-movement with no warning — the A400M scene is partially anticipated. Radio chatter establishes it coming. Communication switches between French and Dutch and English in the specific way of a Belgian military crew — Belgium's linguistic division means Belgian military units routinely operate in both national languages plus English as a working lingua franca.

"AEGIS, ICARUS Zeven, A400M, we are inbound your position, carrying medical and ammunition resupply, ETA twelve minutes."

Then French between crew members. Then Dutch. Then English again for DAOG coordination. The player has twelve minutes of game time — compressed, but felt — before the aircraft arrives. Long enough to continue the mission's current activity. Long enough to almost forget the inbound call. Long enough that when the sound begins it arrives as something half-expected and still surprising.

THE ARRIVAL

The A400M comes in low — lower than it should be for a transport aircraft, low enough that the player who looks up sees detail. The four propeller discs catching the light. The massive fuselage with ICARUS wing markings and Belgian Air Component roundels. The specific silhouette of an aircraft that is all utility, all capacity, no elegance — and beautiful for being exactly what it is rather than anything more.

It crosses the sky slowly enough that the player can watch the whole passage. No sudden peel-off, no afterburner flash. It simply continues, enormous and unhurried, the engine sound filling everything and then gradually, over thirty seconds, vacating it. The sky after an A400M passage feels emptier than the sky before it.

RADIO — POST-PASS

The ICARUS crew confirming drop zone approach. French between the loadmaster and the pilot. The sound of the cargo ramp opening transmitted accidentally on the coordination frequency — a brief burst of wind noise and crew voices from inside the aircraft — before the channel clears.

Then, from somewhere below the flight path, a ground element that isn't AEGIS — unidentified, whose presence the player only infers from this exchange:

"ICARUS Zeven, confirmed. Come around for second pass, we have a marking panel on the north end."

The A400M banks. Begins its circuit. It will be back. The player's squad continues their mission. The aircraft continues its work. Two operations, parallel, neither aware of the other's full picture. The war being large enough to contain both simultaneously.

THEMATIC FUNCTION

The A400M is carrying something toward the place the squad is trying to protect. The supply and the protection are different expressions of the same purpose. The scene does the same tonal work as the Gripen scene — expanding then contracting the player's sense of scale — but at a completely different speed and register. The Gripen scene is a three-second shock. The A400M passage is a thirty-second weight.

SVALBARD PLACEMENT NOTE

If placed during the Permafrost mission's approach phase: the A400M coming in over the fjord, enormous against the grey Norwegian sky, before the squad descends into the mission. The aircraft is carrying something toward the AEGIS datacenter that the squad is trying to protect — supply and protection as parallel expressions of the same purpose. The player who watches it cross the Svalbard sky before the mission begins understands AEGIS as an institution rather than just a squad.

SCENE 4 — THE RED DAWN CALLBACK — PARATROOPER DROP [CONCEPT + PARTIAL]
Location: Smoky Mountains, visible from distance
Campaign: American Freedom (Romeo) — Game 2
Placement: Navigation moment in a mission, specific mission TBD

--------

CONCEPT

At a specific point during Romeo's American Freedom campaign, the player — while navigating on foot through terrain — spots in the distance over the Smoky Mountains a formation of enemy paratroopers descending. The parachutes are visible at distance — a deliberate callback to the 1984 film Red Dawn.

DUAL NARRATIVE PURPOSE

1. In-world navigation device — the player sees where the paratroopers are landing and understands instinctively that this is where the mission is heading. No radio prompt, no waypoint, no UI element. The world tells the player where to go.

2. Deliberate visual homage to Red Dawn — designed to be felt rather than stated. No dialogue draws attention to it. The reference should never be made explicit in any in-game element.

DIALOGUE

Minimal by design — this is largely a wordless cinematic moment. Any dialogue that exists around it should be understated and not draw attention to what the player is seeing. A teammate noticing the chutes. A brief, flat acknowledgment. Nothing more.

TRAILER / PROMOTIONAL NOTE

Flagged as strong standalone trailer asset. Suggested treatment: shot opens on the Smoky Mountains peaceful and still in early morning. Then paratroopers begin to appear — first one, then dozens. Music shift completes the moment. The image works for players who know Red Dawn and works independently as a striking image for those who don't.

STATUS: CONCEPT AND NARRATIVE MECHANICS DEVELOPED. Full dialogue minimal by design and TBD. Needs short development session to finalize any dialogue around the moment and confirm mission placement.

SCENE 5 — THE KERAUNOS URBAN PRECISION STRIKE [FULL TREATMENT]
Aircraft: Dassault Rafale B (x2) — KERAUNOS Flight, French Air and Space Force
DAOG Designation: KERAUNOS
Weapons: AASM Hammer precision air-to-ground munitions
Campaign: Persistent Threat (Ducky) — Game 2
Location: Urban / built-up environment in Western Europe (specific city TBD)

--------

CONTEXT AND NARRATIVE SETUP

Ducky and AEGIS Squad are pinned down by a fortified Russian or enemy position in a dense urban environment — a reinforced building, a blocked street corridor, a bridge approach, or a key infrastructure chokepoint. Too heavily defended for AEGIS to take by direct assault. Ducky or Lt. Tara Halliday (ORACLE) calls in the strike through DAOG Command channels. Colonel Mark Walker (ARES) approves. KERAUNOS FLIGHT acknowledges and begins their attack run. The player, as Ducky, is ordered to pull back to a safe observation distance and hold.

THE STRIKE SEQUENCE — FULL SCENE

[The squad takes cover behind a low wall or in a doorway with a clear line of sight to the target building approximately 200-300 metres ahead. The street between them and the target is empty — rubble, abandoned vehicles, broken glass. It is quiet for a moment, the kind of quiet that precedes something.]

DUCKY:
(into radio, clipped and dry)
KERAUNOS FLIGHT, this is AEGIS ACTUAL. Target is marked. You are clear hot. How copy?

KERAUNOS LEAD:
(French-accented English, calm and professional, slight static on the radio)
AEGIS ACTUAL, KERAUNOS LEAD. Copy clear hot. Tally the mark. Running in now. Stand by.

[A beat. Silence. Then — heard before seen — a rising, tearing sound from the west. High and fast. Both Rafales come in low and fast over the rooftops, in close formation, wings loaded with AASM Hammer munitions, the Thales Damoclès targeting pods visible under the fuselage. They are moving at speed — not the slow, gravitational presence of the AC-130 or the A400M but something faster, more violent, more precise. The noise arrives a half-second after the aircraft, a characteristic shriek of twin engines at military power at low altitude.]

[The first aircraft releases. Two AASM Hammer munitions drop, their propulsion kits igniting almost immediately, guiding them on their terminal approach to the target with GPS and infrared precision. The Rafale pulls up sharply, climbing away to the left.]

[A fraction of a second later — impact. The target position disappears inside two near-simultaneous explosions. Not a massive fireball — these are precision munitions, tightly placed. The building does not collapse dramatically. Its upper floors blow outward and inward. The reinforced position on the ground floor ceases to exist. Dust and smoke billow outward and upward in a dense column.]

[The second Rafale completes a follow-on pass, releasing a single additional round on a secondary position at the building's flank. Another precise impact. Another column of smoke.]

[Both aircraft pull up hard into the sky, their delta-wing planforms clean against the grey European overcast, engines still pushing hard. Within seconds they are climbing away, already a mile out, their shapes shrinking against the clouds.]

KERAUNOS LEAD:
(radio, unhurried)
AEGIS ACTUAL, KERAUNOS LEAD. Splash two. Target destroyed. BDA to follow. KERAUNOS FLIGHT is RTB. Bonne chance.

DUCKY:
(beat — watching the smoke column rise above the buildings ahead)
...Yeah. Thanks, Pierre.

[He stands, checks his weapon, looks at the squad.]

DUCKY:
(quiet, to the squad)
Alright. Let's go see what's left.

[AEGIS moves forward through the settling dust toward the destroyed position.]

VISUAL AND TONAL NOTES

The Rafale's character is defined by SPEED and PRECISION rather than scale. Fast, surgical, gone before the player fully processes it. Under 20 seconds from first visual contact to departure. The AASM Hammer impacts are precise and contained — devastating to the specific target, not spreading destruction across the surrounding street. A scalpel, not a hammer. The Rafale at low altitude produces a distinctive high-pitched twin-engine shriek very different from the turboprop rumble of the A400M or AC-130. Sound arrives slightly after the aircraft. Ducky's "Yeah. Thanks, Pierre." — deliberately casual. This is Ducky. The French pilot's "Bonne chance." — natural and unforced, a French pilot signing off in his own language.

CONTINUITY NOTE: This scene and Scene 6 (Zubr Landing) are causally linked — ARES tasks KERAUNOS at the end of the Zubr sequence. Seeding KERAUNOS assets in earlier missions recommended before this moment lands.

SCENE 6 — THE ZUBR LANDING [FULL TREATMENT]
Vehicle: Zubr-class LCAC (x2 confirmed inbound, x1 third behind, Ropucha standing off)
Campaign: Do Right, Fear None (Ducky) — Game 1
Location: Dutch coastline near the Maasvlakte terminal, Rotterdam
Context: Pre-final mission escalation sequence

--------

CONTEXT AND NARRATIVE SETUP

AEGIS Squad is moving toward the Maasvlakte terminal — approaching through a coastal industrial area, exiting a treeline at the edge of dune terrain, or cresting an elevated road embankment that gives a sudden unobstructed view of the North Sea coastline. This is not a combat encounter. AEGIS watches and withdraws. The Russian amphibious force is too large to engage directly. The player sees what is coming and understands with complete clarity what the final mission is about to be.

THE SEQUENCE — FULL SCENE

[The squad moves along the edge of a coastal road or dune ridge. Industrial structures of the port complex are visible to the right. To the left, the North Sea. Grey sky, grey water, a cold Dutch coastal morning. Wind off the water. Flat, open, nowhere to hide.]

[Frost stops. He raises his rifle — not to shoot, but to look through the scope. He says nothing for a moment.]

FROST:
(quiet, almost to himself)
...Contact. Bearing two-seven-zero. Multiple surface contacts. Large.

[Ducky raises his own optics. What he sees through the scope — and what the camera then cuts to in a wide establishing shot — stops him cold.]

[Two Zubr-class LCACs are coming in from the northwest, riding low and fast over the grey water toward the Dutch beach below the terminal approach. They are enormous — 57 metres long, riding on a cushion of air and spray, their five gas turbine engines producing a sound unlike anything the squad has heard before: a deep, resonant, mechanical howl, felt in the chest before it's fully registered by the ears. The air cushion skirts billow white beneath them. The twin 30mm gun turrets forward are clearly visible, traversing slowly. The cargo ramps are still closed.]

[Behind the two lead Zubrs, further out to sea, more shapes are visible in the grey — a third Zubr, and beyond it the silhouette of a Ropucha-class landing ship standing off approximately two kilometres out.]

LUPIN:
(flat)
How many?

DUCKY:
(still looking through optics)
Two confirmed inbound. Third behind them. There's a Ropucha standing off — maybe two klicks out.

SWITCHBLADE:
(low)
What are they carrying?

DUCKY:
(beat)
Whatever they brought, it fits three tanks. Each.

[Silence. The squad watches. The Zubrs are closing fast — at 35 knots they cover the remaining distance in under two minutes. As they hit the shallow water approach to the beach the air cushion allows them to transition seamlessly from water to shore without slowing — they do not beach themselves the way conventional landing craft do. They simply continue forward, riding the cushion over the sand and gravel of the Dutch shoreline, the massive ramps dropping forward with a sound like a controlled explosion.]

[From the first Zubr's ramp: a T-80 main battle tank rolls forward onto Dutch soil, engine already running, followed immediately by a second. Infantry pour out from the sides — Russian Naval Infantry in full combat kit, spreading left and right into a perimeter, weapons up.]

[From the second Zubr: BTR-82 armoured vehicles rolling down the ramp in sequence, troops dismounting immediately and forming up.]

[The Grad rocket launchers — retracted during transit — are already beginning to elevate on one of the Zubrs, preparing to provide fire support for the landing force.]

[Ducky lowers his optics. He looks at the squad. Nobody speaks for a moment.]

DUCKY:
(to radio, steady)
ARES, this is AEGIS ACTUAL. We have eyes on a Russian amphibious landing — Zubr-class, at least two confirmed, third inbound. Armour and Naval Infantry on the beach. Grid to follow. How copy?

WALKER (ARES):
(radio, controlled)
AEGIS ACTUAL, ARES. Copy all. Hold your position. Do not engage. KERAUNOS is being tasked. Get me that grid.

DUCKY:
(already moving)
Frost. Grid.

FROST:
(already reading it off)
Copy.

[The squad pulls back from the ridge line, moving fast and low. Behind them, the sound of the Zubr turbines continues — that deep mechanical howl carrying across the flat Dutch coastal terrain, audible long after the craft themselves are out of sight. The Grad launchers fire — a rippling series of rockets arcing inland toward the terminal perimeter.]

[The final mission has begun whether AEGIS is ready or not.]

VISUAL AND TONAL NOTES

The Zubr's defining characteristic is its sheer improbability — something that large should not move that fast or transition from water to land that seamlessly. The scene is a pressure indicator, not a combat sequence — AEGIS watches and withdraws. The Ropucha standing off implies more coming. This is not the full picture. The Grad rockets firing at the terminal at the end is the transition moment — the final mission has started on the enemy's terms. Frost's first line — "...Contact. Bearing two-seven-zero. Multiple surface contacts. Large." — is the scene's ignition point. The word "Large" doing the work of an entire paragraph. This scene and Scene 5 (KERAUNOS) are causally linked — ARES tasks KERAUNOS at the end of this sequence.

SCENE 7 — THE AC-130 SPECTRE CAS STRIKE [FULL TREATMENT]
Aircraft: AC-130 Spectre Gunship — SPECTRE FOUR ONE
Campaign: American Freedom (Romeo) — Game 2
Placement: TBD — tentatively late campaign, vehicle exfiltration under pursuit, possibly Frank rescue aftermath from Harlan

--------

CONCEPT

The scene lands with maximum impact because the player has earned it through sustained combat pressure. It is not spectacle inserted into a mission — it is the payoff for the player's effort. The AC-130 arrives because Romeo built something worth arriving for. The central image: Romeo on a mounted weapon in a vehicle moving downhill on a Kentucky county road, the hill's crest creating a natural horizon ahead. The AC-130 rises above that horizon line, flying toward the camera. Just before it reaches maximum visual presence it deploys flares — the iconic angel wing countermeasure smoke trail — flies through its own smoke, and continues.

PLAYER POSITION

Romeo is on a mounted weapon — M2 or similar — in the back of a pickup truck or similar vehicle moving fast downhill. The player has active control of the weapon throughout. The vehicle is moving; the player is managing the pursuit. The mounted weapon position gives the player the elevated sightline needed to see the horizon reveal when it happens.

TACTICAL CONTEXT

The AFC element is in a hot exfiltration — pursuit vehicles behind them, a county road descending a hill with open sky visible ahead. The situation is controlled but uncomfortable. A CAS request went up through Romeo's command two hours earlier. Whether it got processed and approved — whether an aircraft was actually tasked and launched — is genuinely uncertain. This uncertainty is the scene's dramatic engine.

DINGO:
(from the front of the vehicle)
"Command says SPECTRE is en route. Command also said that forty minutes ago."

RADIO SEQUENCE

SPECTRE FOUR ONE makes contact:

"ROMEO element, SPECTRE FOUR ONE, we are at your push frequency. Authenticate."

"ROMEO element authenticates KILO TANGO SEVEN. We have you loud and clear. Be advised we are mobile, south on county route twelve, taking fire from two to three pursuit vehicles, over."

"ROMEO element, copy. SPECTRE FOUR ONE is at angels two, four minutes out. We have your position on ROVER. Stand by, over."

Four minutes. The vehicle accelerates. The pursuit closes slightly on the descent. Romeo fires suppression bursts — not to destroy, to slow. To buy the four minutes.

On radio — F-16 tactical net bleeding briefly into Romeo's receiver, cross-channel bleed from congested airspace during active engagement:

"VIPER flight, BANDIT ONE is turning. He's coming back around—"
"VIPER TWO, I have him, stay with BANDIT TWO—"
"Fox two—"
"[Static]"

Then SPECTRE FOUR ONE again, calm, the specific calm of an AC-130 crew that has been doing this for a very long time:

"ROMEO element, SPECTRE FOUR ONE. One minute. We are in your twelve o'clock. You should have us shortly."

THE REVEAL — THE HORIZON

The player, on the mounted weapon, naturally looks forward — toward the horizon ahead, toward the clean line of the hill's descent against the open sky. Nothing yet. The pursuit vehicles still behind. Still closing. Thirty seconds.

Then — from the direction of the horizon ahead — a sound. Low. Pervasive. Not the sharp crack of jet engines but something broader, something with physical weight — four turboprop engines at combat power. Less a sound than a condition of the air. The player feels it before they see it.

The horizon line ahead — the clean edge of the hill's descent against the sky — begins to change. Something is rising above it. Something enormous.

THE AC-130 CRESTS THE HORIZON

Not fast — nothing about an AC-130 in flight reads as fast, because the aircraft is so large that its speed becomes relative to its size rather than to anything human-scaled. It rises above the hill's horizon the way a ship rises above the curvature of the ocean — slowly, massively, inevitable.

It is flying toward the vehicle. Toward the player. Growing in the frame as it descends toward them on its attack approach. The four propeller discs catching the light. The fuselage enormous and grey and bristling with sensor pods and weapons systems. The 105mm howitzer on the port side fires. The player doesn't see the round. They see the result: the lead pursuit vehicle disappearing in a column of fire and debris fifty metres behind the player's truck.

Then the second vehicle. Gone.

The AC-130 passes directly overhead. For a moment the aircraft fills the frame — a ceiling of grey fuselage and propeller noise and the smell of exhaust. Then past, banking slowly to the left, beginning its circuit.

THE FLARES — THE ANGEL WING

A Su-30 that has been tracking the tactical area for the past ninety seconds locks the AC-130. The aircraft's threat detection system responds immediately. The flares deploy — not one or two but a full defensive salvo, arcing outward from both wingtips simultaneously in the specific spread pattern that gives the countermeasure its silhouette.

CAMERA NOTE — THE FOUR SECONDS

The moment the flares deploy, the camera stops tracking the vehicle entirely and simply looks up. Not dramatically, not with a sudden cut — it drifts upward the way a person's attention drifts upward when something overhead demands it, then holds. The vehicle continues moving beneath the frame. The pursuit is irrelevant. The road is irrelevant.

For approximately four seconds, the only thing in the frame is the AC-130 and the flares and the smoke and the Kentucky sky. Four seconds is a long time in an action sequence. The player retains control of the mounted weapon during it — they could technically be doing something tactical. But they won't be. Nobody will be. Because what's overhead is more important than anything on the road.

That involuntary cessation of tactical thinking is the scene's real achievement. For four seconds, Romeo stops being a soldier and becomes a witness. The player stops playing and starts watching.

Then the smoke begins to diffuse. The aircraft banks away. The road comes back. The mission continues.

RESOLUTION

The Su-30 pulls up hard. The flares have done their work. The missile seeker confused and then lost. VIPER ONE back on him. The engagement moves north — away from the road, away from the vehicle, away from the AC-130 which is now banking slowly, enormously, to come back around for another attack pass if needed.

"ROMEO element, SPECTRE FOUR ONE. BANDITS are egressing. You are clear. What else do you need, over?"

A beat. Romeo looks at the road ahead.

"SPECTRE FOUR ONE, I think we're good. How copy?"

"ROMEO element, copy good. SPECTRE FOUR ONE will remain on station. You call, we answer. Over."

"Thank you, SPECTRE FOUR ONE."

A brief pause. Then, from the AC-130:

"Don't mention it. American Freedom, over."

THE 'AMERICAN FREEDOM' LINE

Not a triumphant callback. Not a winking acknowledgment. The specific thing that a crew says when they know who they're talking to and want them to know they know. The AC-130 crew has been following the AFC's story. Every American service member in the theater knows the name — because Crodyn put it on television.

The crew saying it back to Romeo — over a military radio net, on the record, using the designation that the President of the United States just called a terrorist organization — is a small act of institutional defiance that costs the crew nothing in the moment and means everything to the player who has lived inside this campaign.

It also means something for the wider story: Romeo's command will hear that exchange. Crodyn's intelligence apparatus, monitoring military communications, will hear it too. The AC-130 crew just put themselves on the wrong side of a presidential designation, knowingly, over an open tactical frequency. They knew what they were doing. That is the point.

CLOSING IMAGE

The vehicle continues down the road. Behind them, the smoke trail from the flares is still visible — the angel wing silhouette slowly diffusing in the morning air, the shape holding longer than seems physically reasonable, as if the sky is reluctant to let it go. Romeo watches it from the mounted weapon. The radio continues its work. The mission continues its work. The smoke fades. The AC-130 banks and holds its orbit, patient, overhead.

SCENE 1 — THE AC-130 FLYOVER CHASE (Romeo's Campaign, Game 2 — American Freedom)
Aircraft: AC-130 Gunship
Location: Harlan County, Kentucky (near Calloway and Redbud)
Mission context: Following the rescue of Aldrich / late campaign sequence

Description:
After freeing Aldrich, Romeo's team is in a vehicle being pursued by Russian forces through the Kentucky countryside. The pursuit vehicles are UAZ-type light trucks with mounted guns — fast enough to maintain pressure, not fast enough to fully close the gap on Romeo's vehicle running at full speed. Multiple vehicles in pursuit, at least one with an active mounted gun continuing to engage the player throughout the chase.

The image of a slow, enormous turboprop military gunship appearing at low altitude over a typical Kentucky rural landscape — fields, farmhouses, winding roads — is deliberately jarring and cinematic.

Narrative mechanic / how the pilots got there:

SCENE 5 — KERAUNOS URBAN PRECISION STRIKE (Ducky's Campaign, Game 2 — Persistent Threat)
Aircraft: Dassault Rafale B (x2)
DAOG Designation: KERAUNOS
Nationality: French Air and Space Force
Role: Close Air Support / Precision Ground Attack
Weapons: AASM Hammer precision air-to-ground munitions
Location: Urban / built-up environment in Western Europe (specific city TBD)

Context and narrative setup:
Ducky and AEGIS Squad are pinned down by a fortified Russian or enemy position in a dense urban environment — a reinforced building, a blocked street corridor, a bridge approach, or a key infrastructure chokepoint. Too heavily defended for AEGIS to take by direct assault. Ducky or Lt. Tara Halliday (ORACLE) calls in the strike through DAOG Command channels. Colonel Mark Walker (ARES) approves. KERAUNOS FLIGHT acknowledges and begins their attack run. The player, as Ducky, is ordered to pull back to a safe observation distance and hold.

---

THE STRIKE SEQUENCE — FULL SCENE:

[The squad takes cover behind a low wall or in a doorway with a clear line of sight to the target building approximately 200-300 metres ahead. The street between them and the target is empty — rubble, abandoned vehicles, broken glass. It is quiet for a moment, the kind of quiet that precedes something.]

DUCKY:
(into radio, clipped and dry)
KERAUNOS FLIGHT, this is AEGIS ACTUAL. Target is marked. You are clear hot. How copy?

KERAUNOS LEAD:
(French-accented English, calm and professional, slight static on the radio)
AEGIS ACTUAL, KERAUNOS LEAD. Copy clear hot. Tally the mark. Running in now. Stand by.

[A beat. Silence. Then — heard before seen — a rising, tearing sound from the west. High and fast. Both Rafales come in low and fast over the rooftops, in close formation, wings loaded with AASM Hammer munitions, the Thales Damoclès targeting pods visible under the fuselage. They are moving at speed — not the slow, gravitational presence of the AC-130 or the A400M but something faster, more violent, more precise. The noise arrives a half-second after the aircraft, a characteristic shriek of twin engines at military power at low altitude.]

[The first aircraft releases. Two AASM Hammer munitions drop, their propulsion kits igniting almost immediately, guiding them on their terminal approach to the target with GPS and infrared precision. The Rafale pulls up sharply, climbing away to the left.]

[A fraction of a second later — impact. The target position disappears inside two near-simultaneous explosions. Not a massive fireball — these are precision munitions, tightly placed. The building does not collapse dramatically. Its upper floors blow outward and inward. The reinforced position on the ground floor ceases to exist. Dust and smoke billow outward and upward in a dense column.]

[The second Rafale completes a follow-on pass, releasing a single additional round on a secondary position at the building's flank. Another precise impact. Another column of smoke.]

[Both aircraft pull up hard into the sky, their delta-wing planforms clean against the grey European overcast, engines still pushing hard. Within seconds they are climbing away, already a mile out, their shapes shrinking against the clouds.]

KERAUNOS LEAD:
(radio, unhurried)
AEGIS ACTUAL, KERAUNOS LEAD. Splash two. Target destroyed. BDA to follow. KERAUNOS FLIGHT is RTB. Bonne chance.

DUCKY:
(beat — watching the smoke column rise above the buildings ahead)
...Yeah. Thanks, Pierre.

[He stands, checks his weapon, looks at the squad.]

DUCKY:
(quiet, to the squad)
Alright. Let's go see what's left.

[AEGIS moves forward through the settling dust toward the destroyed position.]

---

Visual and tonal notes:
- The Rafale's character in this scene is defined by SPEED and PRECISION rather than scale. Fast, surgical, and gone before the player has fully processed what happened. The strike is over in under 20 seconds from first visual contact.
- The Rafale's distinctive delta-canard planform is immediately recognizable and unmistakably French. French Air and Space Force roundels and grey/blue-grey livery visible in the brief seconds overhead.
- AASM Hammer impacts: precise, contained, devastating to the specific target. Not spreading destruction across the surrounding street. This is a scalpel, not a hammer.
- Sound design critical. The Rafale at low altitude produces a distinctive high-pitched twin-engine shriek very different from the deep turboprop rumble of the A400M or AC-130. Sound arrives slightly after the aircraft.
- Ducky's "Yeah. Thanks, Pierre." — deliberately casual and characteristically understated. This is Ducky.
- The French pilot's "Bonne chance." — natural and unforced. A French pilot signing off in his own language. Clean and real.
- The column of smoke rising above the European urban skyline is the lasting image of this scene.

Location note: Specific city TBD pending further development of Ducky's Persistent Threat campaign geography. Candidate environments: a Dutch port city, a Baltic coastal city, or a Central European urban centre. Scene is written to be location-agnostic.

Continuity note: KERAUNOS (Dassault Rafale B) confirmed in the DAOG Command force structure under Close Air Support alongside PHAETON (JAS 39 Gripen). Seeding in earlier missions recommended — a radio reference, a mention from ORACLE that KERAUNOS assets are available — before this moment lands.

SCENE 6 — THE ZUBR LANDING (Ducky's Campaign, Game 1 — Do Right, Fear None)
Vehicle: Zubr-class LCAC (x2 confirmed inbound, x1 third behind, Ropucha standing off)
Location: Dutch coastline near the Maasvlakte terminal, Rotterdam
Mission context: Pre-final mission escalation sequence — pressure indicator before the terminal defense mission

Context and narrative setup:
AEGIS Squad is moving toward the Maasvlakte terminal — approaching through a coastal industrial area, exiting a treeline at the edge of dune terrain, or cresting an elevated road embankment that gives a sudden unobstructed view of the North Sea coastline and the shallow beach approaches to the terminal.

This is not a combat encounter. AEGIS watches and withdraws. The Russian amphibious force is too large to engage directly. The player sees what is coming and understands with complete clarity what the final mission is about to be.

---

THE SEQUENCE — FULL SCENE:

[The squad moves along the edge of a coastal road or dune ridge. Industrial structures of the port complex are visible to the right. To the left, the North Sea. Grey sky, grey water, a cold Dutch coastal morning. Wind off the water. Flat, open, nowhere to hide.]

[Frost stops. He raises his rifle — not to shoot, but to look through the scope. He says nothing for a moment.]

FROST:
(quiet, almost to himself)
...Contact. Bearing two-seven-zero. Multiple surface contacts. Large.

[Ducky raises his own optics. What he sees through the scope — and what the camera then cuts to in a wide establishing shot — stops him cold.]

[Two Zubr-class LCACs are coming in from the northwest, riding low and fast over the grey water toward the Dutch beach below the terminal approach. They are enormous — 57 metres long, riding on a cushion of air and spray, their five gas turbine engines producing a sound unlike anything the squad has heard before: a deep, resonant, mechanical howl, felt in the chest before it's fully registered by the ears. The air cushion skirts billow white beneath them. The twin 30mm gun turrets forward are clearly visible, traversing slowly. The cargo ramps are still closed.]

[Behind the two lead Zubrs, further out to sea, more shapes are visible in the grey — a third Zubr, and beyond it the silhouette of a Ropucha-class landing ship standing off approximately two kilometres out.]

LUPIN:
(flat)
How many?

DUCKY:
(still looking through optics)
Two confirmed inbound. Third behind them. There's a Ropucha standing off — maybe two klicks out.

SWITCHBLADE:
(low)
What are they carrying?

DUCKY:
(beat)
Whatever they brought, it fits three tanks. Each.

[Silence. The squad watches. The Zubrs are closing fast — at 35 knots they cover the remaining distance in under two minutes. As they hit the shallow water approach to the beach the air cushion allows them to transition seamlessly from water to shore without slowing — they do not beach themselves the way conventional landing craft do. They simply continue forward, riding the cushion over the sand and gravel of the Dutch shoreline, the massive ramps dropping forward with a sound like a controlled explosion.]

[From the first Zubr's ramp: a T-80 main battle tank rolls forward onto Dutch soil, engine already running, followed immediately by a second. Infantry pour out from the sides — Russian Naval Infantry in full combat kit, spreading left and right into a perimeter, weapons up.]

[From the second Zubr: BTR-82 armoured vehicles rolling down the ramp in sequence, troops dismounting immediately and forming up.]

[The Grad rocket launchers — retracted during transit — are already beginning to elevate on one of the Zubrs, preparing to provide fire support for the landing force.]

[Ducky lowers his optics. He looks at the squad. Nobody speaks for a moment.]

DUCKY:
(to radio, steady)
ARES, this is AEGIS ACTUAL. We have eyes on a Russian amphibious landing — Zubr-class, at least two confirmed, third inbound. Armour and Naval Infantry on the beach. Grid to follow. How copy?

WALKER (ARES):
(radio, controlled)
AEGIS ACTUAL, ARES. Copy all. Hold your position. Do not engage. KERAUNOS is being tasked. Get me that grid.

DUCKY:
(already moving)
Frost. Grid.

FROST:
(already reading it off)
Copy.

[The squad pulls back from the ridge line, moving fast and low. Behind them, the sound of the Zubr turbines continues — that deep mechanical howl carrying across the flat Dutch coastal terrain, audible long after the craft themselves are out of sight. The Grad launchers fire — a rippling series of rockets arcing inland toward the terminal perimeter.]

[The final mission has begun whether AEGIS is ready or not.]

---

Visual and tonal notes:
- The Zubr's defining visual characteristic is its sheer improbability — something that large should not move that fast or transition from water to land that seamlessly. The air cushion skirt billowing white underneath it, the spray trail behind it, the turbine howl — these details make the image unforgettable. Most players will never have seen a Zubr before. This is their introduction to it and it should feel like a revelation.
- The scene is a pressure indicator, not a combat sequence. AEGIS watches and withdraws. The enemy force is too large to engage here — that's the point. The player sees what they're about to fight and feels the weight of it before the mission begins.
- The Ropucha-class landing ship standing off in the background implies more forces in reserve. The Zubrs are the fast forward element. There is more coming. This is not the full picture.
- The Grad rockets firing at the terminal at the end of the sequence is the transition moment — the final mission has started on the enemy's terms. No pause, no preparation time.
- Sound design critical. The Zubr uses five gas turbines — three for propulsion and two for the air cushion lift system, each delivering approximately 12,100 shaft horsepower. The sound at close range on a flat coastal environment would be extraordinary — felt physically as much as heard. Present in the scene from the moment Frost first spots them, growing as they close the distance.
- The Ropucha standing off in the background implies there is more coming behind the Zubrs. The player does not see everything. That incompleteness is part of the dread.
- Frost's first line — "Contact. Bearing two-seven-zero. Multiple surface contacts. Large." — is the scene's emotional ignition point. The word "large" doing the work of an entire paragraph.
- The Dutch coastal terrain — flat, open, industrial, dunes and beach and port infrastructure — gives the scene a specific recognizable geography. The flatness means the Zubrs are visible from a long distance, giving the player time to process what they're seeing before the scale of it fully registers.
- This scene also serves as a continuity bridge to Scene 5 (KERAUNOS) — ARES tasks KERAUNOS assets at the end of this sequence, implying the Rafales are being sent to deal with the landing force. The two scenes are causally linked.

Continuity note:
The Ropucha-class landing ship standing off is a real Russian Navy vessel class used for amphibious operations and is consistent with the Baltic Fleet operational context established in Ducky's Game 1 campaign. The combination of Zubr LCACs for fast shallow-water landing and a Ropucha for heavier equipment delivery is historically accurate Russian amphibious doctrine.

ANGEL — CINEMATIC SCREENPLAY PASS
Project Warriors — Game 2 — Romeo Campaign
Late Act 2 / Exfil Chase Set Piece

CANON LOCK
- Location thread: post-liberation AFC operation tied to Aldridge
- Vehicle: current-generation Ford F-150, quad cab
- Mount: improvised bed-mounted ring / pintle
- Weapon: M240B
- Ammo state: two cans total
- Player position: Romeo on mounted gun
- Pursuit: three enemy UAZ-type vehicles, at least one with mounted gun
- Air support: AC-130 Spectre gunship, callsign SPECTRE FOUR ONE

NOTE
This pass is written as an in-engine cinematic/gameplay hybrid rather than a non-interactive cutscene. The player should remain in control for most of the sequence, especially the reveal.

======================================================================
EXT. KENTUCKY COUNTY ROAD - DAY
======================================================================

A battered FORD F-150 tears downhill along a narrow Kentucky road bordered by pasture fence, brush, and tree line. Dust boils behind it. The truck is civilian at its core, but changed by necessity: improvised mount in the bed, welds visible, metal scraped raw at the joints, gear lashed down wherever it will fit.

ROMEO is on the mounted M240B in the truck bed, braced inside the ring mount, one boot slipping half an inch every time the rear axle kicks sideways over a rut.

The truck is already under fire.

Rounds crack past the cab. A rear window spiders. Fence posts flick past in a strobing rhythm.

                               DRIVER
                 Contact rear! Rear, rear, rear!

                               FRONT PASSENGER
                 They stayed on us!

                               AMMO TEAMMATE
                 Romeo, on the gun!

Romeo hauls the weapon around toward the rear arc.

Down the road behind them: a DUST PLUME, then the first UAZ breaks through it, hard and fast. A second appears on the bend behind it. Then a third, offset wide, rising and falling with the road.

                               FRONT PASSENGER
                 One truck--

                               ALDRIDGE
                 No. Three. Three total.

A burst from the lead vehicle walks up the road and snaps over the truck bed.

                               DRIVER
                 Lead one's mounted!

Romeo settles the sights.

                               AMMO TEAMMATE
                 Romeo! Two cans total!

That lands harder than the incoming fire.

Romeo opens up in short, controlled bursts. The M240B pounds through the chassis. Brass rattles across the truck bed and vanishes into the road behind them.

The lead UAZ shudders under the impacts. The enemy gunner ducks. The windshield crazes.

                               DRIVER
                 Lead gun truck! Kill that gun!

                               ALDRIDGE
                 Don't chase all three. Break the one that can kill us.

The road bends. The truck leans hard. Romeo has to fight the mount and the road at the same time.

                               AMMO TEAMMATE
                 Short bursts!

The second UAZ starts offsetting for angle. The third hangs back just enough to be trouble later.

Romeo bursts again -- this time longer, less clean. The ring mount clatters. The truck drops into a washout and slams out of it. The weapon bucks off target.

                               DRIVER
                 Hold on!

                               ALDRIDGE
                 Road drops here!

The lead gun truck recovers and answers with another burst. A round punches through thin sheet metal somewhere behind Romeo with an ugly, intimate sound.

Romeo keeps firing.

The belt runs dry.

A dead click.

For one sharp instant the whole chase becomes enormous.

                               ROMEO / AMMO TEAMMATE
                 Reloading!

                               DRIVER
                 Make it fast!

                               ALDRIDGE
                 They're gaining!

Romeo ducks into the ring. The feed cover comes up. The AMMO TEAMMATE wrestles the next can upward from the cab/bed handoff position, one hand braced, one hand fighting the vibration of the truck.

                               AMMO TEAMMATE
                 Hold it steady -- hold it steady--

                               DRIVER
                 I am!

The truck hits another bump. The belt slips. Romeo reseats it with a hard slap, slams the cover shut, charges the weapon, and comes back up on the gun just as the second UAZ closes the gap.

He fires again. The second belt starts to disappear.

The middle phase of the chase is worse than the beginning. The Russians are not just chasing. They are learning the truck's rhythm, its weak windows, its bad angles.

                 Stay on the road!

Nobody would.

                 SPECTRE FOUR ONE, I think we're good. How copy?

                               SPECTRE FOUR ONE (RADIO)

                               FRONT PASSENGER / ROMEO
                 Thank you, SPECTRE FOUR ONE.

                               SPECTRE FOUR ONE (RADIO)
                 Don't mention it. American Freedom, over.

                               ALDRIDGE
                 Yeah.

                               ALDRIDGE
                 Hell of a thing.

DESIGN GUARDRAILS
----------------------------------------------------------------
1. Do not let the overlook beat move too fast.
   The dread comes from looking long enough to not understand.

2. Do not add explicit tech exposition.
   No one should say “PANDORA,” “autonomous system,” or anything intelligence-flavored.

3. Do not add nearby drones or gunfire before the road impact sequence.
   The threat should remain indeterminate through most of the scene.

4. Keep panic compressed once it starts.
   The return to the car should feel messy and urgent, not like a full survival level.

5. The road impacts are the thesis image.
   The airliner is shock; the cars are recognition.

6. The analog SUV must start cleanly.
   That moment matters thematically.

7. Cut on Louis’ “No.”
   Do not continue into another action beat.
