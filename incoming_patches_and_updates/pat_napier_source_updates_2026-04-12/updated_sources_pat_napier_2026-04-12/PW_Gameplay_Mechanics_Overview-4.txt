01. CAMERA / PERSPECTIVE FORMAT

STATUS: Locked for Game 1. Revisit for later games in trilogy.

Project Warriors Game 1 is a first-person shooter. FPS perspective is
maintained throughout gameplay. Third-person perspective is reserved
exclusively for cinematics where visual framing demands it — story moments,
character reveals, dramatic scene compositions. This is not a hybrid
gameplay system.

Rationale: The narrative prompt system, the armor and hit zone system,
and the tactical immersion of the game all function optimally in first person.
Third-person gameplay would dilute the effect of all three. Building authentic
dual-perspective gameplay would also represent significant additional scope
inappropriate for a first entry in the trilogy.

Revisit note: Later games in the trilogy may explore switchable first and
third person depending on how the story and scope develop.

02. NARRATIVE PROMPT SYSTEM

STATUS: Pinned for full development. Core concept confirmed and locked.

OVERVIEW

UI action prompts — the text that appears on screen to tell the player what
button to press and what action it performs — are used as a subtle narrative
device reflecting the psychology of the character through whose eyes the
player is looking. The prompt text is not purely functional. In strategically
selected moments it carries emotional and tonal information about the
character's mental state, moral position, or situational register.

CORE PRINCIPLE

This mechanic works only through restraint. The majority of prompts
throughout the game are functional and unremarkable — they do their job
invisibly. The narrative-laden prompts are placed sparingly at specific
inflection points: tonal shifts, moral thresholds, psychologically significant
moments. Their power comes entirely from the contrast with the surrounding
functional prompts. If every prompt attempts to be meaningful, none of them
are.

EXAMPLE — CONFIRMED

Cold Frontier flashback mission (Frost). During the QTE sequence in which
Frost kills the Vympel operator at the lake shore with his knife, the prompt
reads:

    DISPATCH

Not "stab." Not "kill." Not "neutralize." The clinical neutrality of "dispatch"
reflects the psychological state Frost is in at that moment — not raging,
not hesitating, already decided. A trained professional whose mind has gone
somewhere very quiet and very cold. The word communicates his interior
state more efficiently than any line of dialogue could.

CHARACTER-SPECIFIC PROMPT VOCABULARIES

Each protagonist has a consistent prompt vocabulary that reflects their
identity and cognitive register. These vocabularies must remain consistent
throughout the game so that players begin to feel the difference between
campaigns without being told about it.

Romeo — direct, physical, soldierly:
"hit," "push," "go," "move," "take it"

Spook — clinical, operational, intelligence-inflected:
"neutralize," "acquire," "extract," "secure," "eliminate"

Frost — neutral, detached, professionally cold:
"dispatch," "remove," "clear," "hold," "acquire"

Lupin — direct, physically charged, protective:
"break it," "push them back," "hold the line," "cover"

SHIFTING PROMPTS

In specific dramatic moments the prompt text can change while the player
is reading it, reflecting a sudden shift in the situational or tonal setup
of the scene. Example: a prompt that reads "spare" then shifts to "end it"
before the player acts communicates a character's resolve forming without
a single line of dialogue. This device must be used extremely sparingly —
it is a high-impact tool that loses force through overuse.

MORAL THRESHOLD APPLICATION

The most powerful application of this system is using a prompt change to
signal a moral line being crossed without the game editorializing about it.
If a prompt that has read "subdue" throughout a mission suddenly reads
"kill" in a specific moment, the player feels that shift before they have
consciously processed it.

PLACEMENT PHILOSOPHY

Prompts doing narrative work are placed at:
— Psychologically significant character moments
— Tonal or register shifts in the story
— Moral thresholds the player is crossing
— Moments of dramatic irony where the character's interior state
  contrasts with the external situation

03. PERSPECTIVE TRANSITION MECHANIC (FROST / LUPIN)

STATUS: Core concept confirmed and locked. Mission applications in
development. Full standalone document also exists in project knowledge:
PW_Transition_Mechanic_Overview.txt

OVERVIEW

In Ducky's campaign missions, the player may temporarily shift perspective
and control from Ducky to Frost (SSGT Aki Peltola, TRG M10 precision rifle)
or Lupin (Ştefan Dragavei, Scar-H DMR). These are scripted, mid-mission
perspective shifts with no loading screen. They are not player-triggered
on demand. The tactical situation must credibly require that specific
operator's capability.

TRANSITION IN

Triggered by a radio call from Ducky or a command element directed at
the operator by name. Functional and mission-driven — never expository.
World audio pulls slightly. Camera performs a directional zoom or lift
toward the operator's elevated position. Operator's response heard during
or just after the perspective settles. Player has control immediately.

FROST SECTIONS

Methodical, deliberate, long-range. Pre-shot routine applies: rangefinder,
environmental reading, stability, then shot. Sparse flat communication.
Clinical prompt vocabulary. Longer duration than Lupin sections. Limited
high-value target count — not volume suppression.

LUPIN SECTIONS

Pressurized, faster-paced, closer range. Intercepting threats moving toward
the squad. Direct, physically charged communication. Aggressive prompt
vocabulary. Shorter duration than Frost sections — intense and decisive.

TRANSITION OUT

Operator sign-off when section objective is achieved. Brief and functional.
Ducky's voice may be heard just before the perspective returns so the
player begins reorienting before the shift completes.

Frost example: "Clear. You're good to move."
Lupin example: "Go. I've got the rear."

CONFIRMED MISSION APPLICATION

Maasvlakte Oil and Gas Terminal — Ducky's Campaign, Game 1, Final Mission.
Frost transition mid-mission (long-range threat / Severomorsk fire support
problem). Lupin transition late-mission (close surge following NMESIS launch).
Ducky bookends the mission — opens it and closes it through to extraction.

04. INJURY SYSTEM

STATUS: Pinned. Awaiting full development.

OVERVIEW

Regenerating health (Call of Duty style — step out of fire, health refills
automatically) is not appropriate for the game's realistic difficulty mode.
On maximum realism settings, health does not regenerate. On lower difficulty
settings, some form of regeneration or assisted recovery may be available
as an accessibility option.

CORE TENSION TO RESOLVE

Resource vs. consequence model. Does getting shot cost something permanent
within a mission (reduced capacity, slower movement, limited healing uses),
or does it temporarily impair until treated? These lead to fundamentally
different gameplay feels. The answer likely sits on a spectrum tunable
by difficulty setting.

PER-CHARACTER DIFFERENTIATION

Romeo — resistance cell operator in occupied Kentucky. Limited and
improvised access to medical supplies. Scavenging dependent.

Ducky — NATO SOF with squad logistics. Has Switchblade (Cassandra Stewart)
as an embedded combat medic. Best supported of the three protagonists.
Switchblade's role as medic should be mechanically meaningful, not
just narrative flavor.

Spook — CIA Ground Branch with agency logistics behind him. Better
supplied than Romeo but operating in more isolated conditions than Ducky.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Injury states (what conditions are possible beyond simple damage)
— Healing method and how it is performed
— Healing frequency and resource limits
— Per-character access to care and supplies
— Realism spectrum — what changes across difficulty settings
— Interaction with the hit zone system (see section 05)

05. HIT ZONE / ARMOR COVERAGE SYSTEM

STATUS: Pinned. Awaiting full development. Closely coupled with
Injury System (04) and Enemy Tactical Sophistication Tiers (06).

OVERVIEW

An anatomical hit zone model layered over a realistic armor coverage map.
Rather than a unified health bar, damage is location-specific and armor
coverage is geometrically accurate to real plate carrier and body armor
designs. This forces the player to think tactically about shot placement
rather than defaulting to center-mass fire.

PELVIC GIRDLE TARGETING

Against a target wearing Level IV plates covering the thoracic cavity, the
exposed pelvis becomes the primary target for a trained shooter. A pelvic
girdle hit is vascularly catastrophic and immediately degrades mobility
even if not immediately fatal. This is real doctrine and building it into
the game teaches authentic tactical thinking while grounding encounters
in physical reality.

T-ZONE AND FLACCID PARALYSIS TARGETING

Additional advanced techniques employed by trained special operations
personnel against high-protection targets. These and similar techniques
are reserved for high-tier enemies and for the player on maximum realism
settings. To be fully developed during the hit zone system development pass.

ARMOR COVERAGE GEOMETRY

Coverage varies meaningfully by faction, character, and situation:

— Romeo in a resistance cell: improvised or scavenged armor, significant
  coverage gaps
— Ducky's AEGIS operators: NATO-spec full plate carrier coverage
— Spook: mission-dependent, variable
— Russian Spetsnaz / Vympel: high-tier military spec
— DPRK motorized troops: mid-tier military
— Cartel sicarios: variable, often minimal
— Vympel combat divers (Wirecutter mission): specialized, different profile

DIFFICULTY SCALING

On maximum realism: full anatomical targeting model active for both
player and enemies. On lower settings: system may be simplified,
assisted, or partially abstracted.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Full anatomical hit zone map
— Armor tier and coverage geometry per faction and character
— Interaction with injury system (what does a pelvic hit cause vs.
  a limb hit vs. a plate strike)
— Advanced technique implementation (pelvic, T-zone, etc.)
— Difficulty scaling model

06. ENEMY TACTICAL SOPHISTICATION TIERS

STATUS: Pinned. Awaiting full development. Directly coupled with
Hit Zone / Armor Coverage System (05).

OVERVIEW

Enemy combatants operate according to a tactical sophistication tier system
that maps to their real-world training level. Higher-tier enemies employ
advanced shooting techniques — including exploiting armor coverage gaps —
that lower-tier enemies do not know or use deliberately.

TIER STRUCTURE (DRAFT)

Tier 1 — Basic / Irregular
Cartel enforcers, conscripts, irregular militia. Shoot at center mass.
May suppress. Will not deliberately exploit armor gaps. Pelvic hits
from this tier are incidental, not intentional.

Tier 2 — Trained Military
Regular Russian infantry, DPRK motorized troops. Basic fire discipline,
movement and shooting, cover use. Not running advanced techniques
but tactically coherent.

Tier 3 — Special Operations / Elite
Spetsnaz, Vympel combat divers, FSB operators, War Wolves. Know and
use pelvic girdle targeting, T-zone shots, and other techniques that
exploit the geometry of high-level armor. Fighting this tier requires
the player to think defensively about their own armor coverage.

ARMOR PROGRESSION INTERACTION

As higher-tier enemies become more prevalent deeper into the game,
the player gains access to better armor coverage options. Those better
options then require Tier 3 enemies to adapt further — targeting newly
exposed gaps or switching techniques. This creates a satisfying
escalating dynamic across the game's progression arc.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Full tier definitions with specific enemy types assigned
— Technique library per tier
— AI behavior implementation for gap exploitation
— Player armor progression tied to enemy tier escalation
— Difficulty scaling — on lower settings, Tier 3 enemies may
  not employ advanced techniques against the player

07. AMMO MANAGEMENT / MAGAZINE RETENTION

STATUS: Pinned. Awaiting full development.

OVERVIEW

On realistic difficulty settings, magazine management is tracked and
consequential. When the player reloads before a magazine is fully spent,
the partial magazine is retained in their pouch rather than discarded.
Reloading then draws the next available magazine, which may not be full.
The player must track their magazine states across an engagement.

MAGAZINE CONSOLIDATION

The player can consolidate ammunition from partial magazines into fuller
ones at the cost of time and vulnerability — they cannot shoot while doing
this. This mirrors a real technique used by shooters during lulls in contact.

Design question to resolve: Is consolidation a manual player-initiated
field action, something performed only at safe zones or workbenches, or
available anywhere at significant risk cost? All three have different
gameplay implications.

PER-CHARACTER AMMO ECONOMY

Romeo — scrounging from fallen enemies and supply caches in occupied
Kentucky. Enemy ammo compatibility is a significant resource consideration.
Russian 7.62 ammo becomes commonly available as Russian occupation
forces are encountered — the CMMG Mk47 (rare weapon drop, Down Rodeo
mission) is specifically designed around this, using Russian-caliber ammo.

Ducky — NATO standardized loadouts with squad logistics support.
More reliable resupply but still subject to consumption tracking
on realistic settings.

Spook — Agency logistics behind him but operating in more isolated
conditions. Mission-dependent resupply.

ENEMY AMMO COMPATIBILITY

Enemy weapon calibers as a scavenge resource is an established mechanic
seeded in the narrative (CMMG Mk47 / Russian 7.62 note). This needs
to be systematized — which enemy factions drop which calibers, and
which player weapons can use them.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Magazine state tracking and UI communication to player
— Tactical reload logic and partial mag retention
— Consolidation mechanic — where, when, at what cost
— Ammo scarcity tuning per campaign and difficulty
— Enemy ammo compatibility and scavenge system
— Interaction with per-character logistics differences

08. PRECISION RIFLE / RANGEFINDER MECHANIC

STATUS: Pinned. Early development begun. Coupled with
Perspective Transition Mechanic (03).

OVERVIEW

Frost's TRG M10 precision bolt-action rifle sections operate under a
distinct pre-shot routine mechanic that makes long-range precision shooting
feel fundamentally different from any other shooting in the game. The player
cannot simply shoulder the rifle and fire. Preparation is required and
each step in the routine is a discrete action.

PRE-SHOT ROUTINE (DRAFT)

Step 1 — Rangefinder acquisition
Frost raises the rangefinder as a separate action from shouldering the rifle.
Gets a reading on the target or target area. On maximum realism this feeds
a manual scope adjustment the player must perform. On lower settings
this may be assisted or automated.

Step 2 — Environmental reading
Wind direction and speed affect shot at operational ranges. On lower
difficulty: communicated via subtle environmental cues (foliage movement,
flags, dust) with some UI assistance. On maximum realism: purely
observational, no UI data provided.

Step 3 — Positional stability
Frost must be properly settled before the shot is valid. Rather than a
traditional stability meter or bar, the narrative prompt system handles
this: prompt text shifts as stability increases.

Proposed prompt sequence:
"breathe" → "hold" → "now"

This collapses the UI stability indicator into the character's interior
state, maintaining immersion while communicating the same information.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Full pre-shot routine implementation and player feedback design
— Difficulty scaling for each step
— Scope adjustment mechanic (manual vs. assisted)
— Wind and environmental reading system
— Interaction with Perspective Transition sections
— Spotter question: does Frost work alone or with an NPC
  spotter in overwatch sections (working alone fits his character
  but spotter interaction adds texture)

09. BLACK MARKET MECHANIC

STATUS: Pinned. Awaiting full development.

OVERVIEW

A black market / underground weapons and equipment acquisition system
exists in the game allowing players to obtain weapons, attachments, and
equipment outside of standard mission-issued loadouts. How this is accessed
and what it contains varies by campaign and character.

TWO POSSIBLE ACCESS MODELS

Located contact model — the player finds and unlocks fixers, arms dealers,
and black market nodes in the game world. Physical locations that must
be reached or discovered. Suits Romeo's campaign naturally — scrounging
and trading in occupied Kentucky through resistance network contacts.

Remote access model — black market runs through Spook's secure device
(terminal, laptop, or secure smartphone — to be determined). Suits Spook's
world of back-channel Agency contacts and off-book procurement.

Ducky likely sits between these two models — NATO logistics as the primary
supply chain but with some capacity to source outside it through DAOG
contacts or allied networks.

These could be three different expressions of the same underlying system,
each flavored to match the protagonist's world.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Access model decision per campaign
— What is available through black market vs. standard supply
— Contact discovery and unlock mechanic
— Economy / currency system
— Inventory and carrying capacity implications
— Interaction with ammo management system

10. BODY CARRYING AND DISPOSAL

STATUS: Partially developed. Core design direction confirmed.
Coupled with Stealth System (12), Casualty Extraction /
Retention Lanyard System (19), and Enemy Tactical
Sophistication Tiers (06).

OVERVIEW

In stealth-oriented missions the player must move and conceal
bodies to prevent discovery by remaining enemies. This mechanic
is contextually available — not present in every mission. It appears
where the story and operational context demand it and is absent
where it doesn't fit. The player is never expected to hide bodies
during open combat missions.

CONTEXTUAL AVAILABILITY PER CAMPAIGN

Spook — highest demand. Montoya Farm underground tunnel sequence
is the primary confirmed application. Other covert infiltration
missions in Spook's campaign will also require it. Body disposal
is a core stealth tool for this campaign.

Romeo — not required in Game 1 combat missions. Becomes relevant
in Game 2 and Game 3 resistance cell operations in occupied
territory, where concealing activity from occupying forces is
a survival requirement.

Ducky — required in specific missions. Not a campaign-wide
constant but present in stealth-adjacent operations where
exposure of a killed enemy would compromise the squad's position.

MOVEMENT — INTEGRATION WITH LANYARD SYSTEM

Body movement uses the same lanyard drag system established
in section 19. The carabiner clips to the loop on a dead
enemy's plate carrier or tactical vest and the player drags
backwards with full upper body freedom.

Enemies not wearing compatible equipment — lightly dressed
combatants, civilians, non-tactical personnel — must be
moved by hand drag or over-shoulder carry with compromised
weapon access. This distinction is consistent with the
casualty extraction system and applies to both friendly
casualties and enemy bodies.

DISPOSAL METHODS

Receptacle disposal — primary method. Interactable objects
placed by level designers in tactically relevant locations:
dumpsters, lockers, large containers, vehicle trunks, any
environment-appropriate container. Offers the highest
concealment reliability — a body inside a receptacle will
not be found by standard patrol behavior.

Shadow / dark area concealment — secondary method. Faster
than finding a receptacle but less reliable. A body concealed
in darkness may be found if patrol patterns shift or a light
source is activated. Risk differential versus receptacle
disposal is a meaningful tactical choice.

Environmental disposal — irreversible, highest reliability.
Elevator shafts, pits, water, drops from height. The player
trades positioning effort and approach challenge for the
certainty that the body cannot be found. Available only
where the environment provides the opportunity.

PROXIMITY-BASED DISPOSAL PROMPT

To avoid frustrating alignment requirements — particularly
given that the lanyard drag moves the player backwards only
— disposal uses a proximity radius system rather than
requiring precise positioning at the receptacle.

When the player is within the proximity radius of a valid
disposal point AND the body is also within that radius,
the disposal prompt becomes available. The player unclips
the lanyard, makes any final small adjustment, and the
interaction fires. This untethers level designers from
having to perfectly align receptacle placement with the
backwards drag approach angle while still requiring genuine
effort to reach the disposal point.

Proximity radius must be tuned to feel accessible without
feeling trivial. Tight enough that the player earns the
disposal. Loose enough that minor misalignment doesn't
restart the entire approach. Final tuning is a development
pass task.

Level design constraint: avoid placing receptacles in
cramped or difficult-to-access spaces. These create approach
problems for the drag system and risk players getting stuck.
Receptacles should be reachable with clear backwards
approach angles.

NARRATIVE PROMPT VOCABULARY

Body disposal prompts follow the character-specific vocabulary
established in section 02:

Spook — "dispose" — clinical, operational, the body is
evidence to be managed

Romeo — "move him" — more human register, less sanitized

Ducky — "stash" — direct, military, functional

These are working proposals subject to refinement during
the narrative prompt development pass.

ENEMY DISCOVERY — RICHER MODEL CONFIRMED

The game uses a richer body discovery model rather than
simple line-of-sight detection. Enemy response to a missing
team member escalates through a ladder tied directly to
the tactical sophistication tier of the enemy faction
(see section 06):

Stage 1 — Nothing. The absence is not yet noticed.
Window of opportunity for the player. Duration before
Stage 2 varies by enemy tier — Tier 3 enemies notice
faster than Tier 1.

Stage 2 — Search behavior. One or two enemies break
from patrol pattern to look for the missing man. Alert
state elevated but not fully blown. If nothing is found,
enemies eventually return to patrol with elevated
suspicion — shorter patrol intervals, heightened awareness.

Stage 3 — Escalated response. Triggered by: finding
a concealed body, finding multiple bodies, or finding
a poorly concealed body. Response severity scales
with tactical sophistication tier:

Tier 1 — loose additional search, slow response
Tier 2 — structured search pattern, moderate response
Tier 3 — immediate coordinated response, possible
reinforcement call — scale of reinforcement depends
on what was found and how many bodies

Finding nothing eventually de-escalates. Finding a body
does not. Finding multiple bodies or evidence of a
pattern triggers maximum available response for that tier.

This system rewards thorough concealment without
catastrophically punishing imperfect play at lower tiers.
At Tier 3 the margin for error is genuinely thin.
That asymmetry is intentional.

FULL INTEGRATION NOTE

The discovery escalation ladder must be developed in
conjunction with the tactical sophistication tier system
(section 06) during the dedicated development pass for
both systems. They are tightly coupled and should not
be designed independently.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Proximity radius tuning for disposal prompt
— Receptacle placement guidelines for level designers
— Shadow concealment risk model and light source interaction
— Environmental disposal opportunities per mission
— Enemy discovery escalation ladder full design
— Integration with tactical sophistication tiers (section 06)
— Per-campaign availability and tuning
— Narrative prompt vocabulary finalization
— Montoya Farm full body disposal design pass

11. CLOSE AIR SUPPORT AND EXTRACTION CALLS

STATUS: Pinned. Awaiting full development.

OVERVIEW

The player can call in external support assets — close air support strikes,
extraction vehicles, fire missions — in certain mission contexts. Two broad
implementation models are under consideration.

SCRIPTED SUPPORT ZONE MODEL

Designated areas on the map from which support can be called. Simpler
to balance but feels more artificial and less immersive.

DEVICE / INVENTORY MODEL

Support is called through a device in the player's inventory — a radio,
secure phone, or terminal appropriate to the character. More immersive
and fits the world better. Availability of support becomes a narrative
tool — support goes dark when HOPLON comms are disrupted (already
flagged as a gameplay-relevant narrative event), reflecting the story's
comms-disruption beats in a mechanically meaningful way.

PER-CHARACTER SUPPORT ACCESS

Ducky — richest support network. DAOG assets: PHAETON (JAS 39 Gripen
CAS), KERAUNOS (Dassault Rafale), ARTEMIS (artillery), PEGASUS (extraction
helicopter). Access through DAOG comms structure.

Romeo — most limited and unreliable support. Resistance network contacts,
improvised assets, occasionally US military elements where available.
Support unavailability is itself a narrative statement about his situation.

Spook — Agency channels. Different flavor of support — less military,
more covert. Safe house contacts, extraction vehicles, occasional

12. STEALTH SYSTEM

STATUS: Partially developed through narrative material. Needs
dedicated mechanics development pass.

OVERVIEW

Stealth is a significant and recurring gameplay mode across all three
campaigns, most prominently in Spook's. The existing narrative material
implies a multi-layered detection system with distinct NPC allegiance tiers
and context-sensitive alert states.

CONFIRMED DESIGN ELEMENTS (from narrative material)

Multi-tier NPC awareness — Montoya Farm gala sequence establishes
at least three distinct NPC tiers with different detection behaviors:
— Civilian guests: will not alert security if player is moving normally,
  only react to obviously suspicious behavior
— Facility and security staff: will alert if player acts suspicious or
  enters off-limits areas
— High-value targets (Stockbridge): will immediately alert and fail

— Directional microphone (Montoya Farm — Tripper-issued, used to

— CCTV system access and sabotage (Montoya Farm security room)

— Body carrying and concealment (see section 10)

Stealth-to-action escalation sequences — Vienna apartment sequence
establishes a three-phase escalation: stealth infiltration → discovery
→ parkour chase → motorbike escape → vehicle pursuit with gunfight.
The transition between phases should feel organic rather than abrupt.

Social stealth — Montoya Farm gala provides a social stealth environment
where the player can move among civilians without triggering alert,
using the crowd as partial cover while navigating toward objectives.

13. ENEMY FACTION INTERACTION / DYNAMIC AI ENCOUNTERS

OVERVIEW

Multiple enemy factions can occupy the same space and engage each other

CONFIRMED DESIGN EXAMPLE — DOWN RODEO (Romeo, Game 1)

Three factions in proximity: Romeo's element, Los Sietes cartel (11 men),

17. OPTIONAL MORAL CHOICES WITH NARRATIVE PAYOFF

STATUS: Partially developed through narrative material.
Needs dedicated mechanics development pass.

OVERVIEW

The game contains optional moral decisions that carry no mechanical
penalty or reward but produce narrative consequences — story payoffs
that reward player engagement without punishing non-engagement.

CONFIRMED EXAMPLE — NAVARRE / BUDANOVA DECISION

At the end of the Navarre house infiltration mission (Spook, Game 1),
the player can choose to call in a favor with Langley contacts to
provide Navarre and Budanova with new identities and a fresh start.

Design principle: low mechanical cost, meaningful narrative reward.

no stat change or gameplay advantage. It rewards players who engage
with the world beyond the objective markers.

The decision of how to handle the Los Sietes / DPRK engagement

outcomes and a rare weapon reward for certain choices. This is a
lighter version of the same principle applied to a combat encounter.

19. CASUALTY EXTRACTION / RETENTION LANYARD SYSTEM

STATUS: Confirmed design direction. Partially developed.
Coupled with Injury System (04), Body Carrying and Disposal (10),
and CASEVAC System (20).

OVERVIEW

All three protagonists carry a retention lanyard as standard equipment —
a tool with one snap shackle end and one carabiner end, modeled on
real-world equivalents such as the Warrior Assault Systems retention
lanyard. Its primary non-combat use is safety aboard helicopters and
watercraft, which is why all three characters carry it without it requiring
narrative justification as a combat tool.

In a casualty scenario, the carabiner clips to the loop on the back of
a downed teammate's plate carrier or tactical vest, allowing the player
to drag them hands-free while walking backwards. This is the default
and preferred extraction method — it is technically more advantageous
and less dangerous than hand dragging in almost all situations.

MOVEMENT CONSTRAINTS WHILE DRAGGING

— Direction: backwards only
— Lateral movement: very limited
— Turning radius: very limited
— Speed: significantly reduced, no sprinting
— Upper body: fully free — player can shoot, reload, switch weapons
— Crouching while dragging: physically very difficult; reserved for
  specific QTE moments in awkward terrain or tight spaces

The player is not defenceless while dragging. The tradeoff is mobility
for hands-free teammate extraction. This is a meaningful tactical
decision rather than a binary choice between fighting and saving.

LANYARD LIMITATION — NO COMPATIBLE LOOP

The lanyard system requires a plate carrier or tactical vest with a
drag loop. When the casualty is not wearing compatible equipment
— a civilian, a lightly equipped contact, or a non-tactical character
— the lanyard cannot be used. In these cases the player must use
an over-shoulder carry or hand drag, with compromised weapon access.

CONFIRMED SPECIAL APPLICATION — MONTOYA FARM

The Montoya Farm underground infiltration (Spook, Game 1) contains
a confirmed carry moment where the casualty is not wearing compatible
tactical equipment. The player cannot use the lanyard and must find
an alternative carry method. This variation is narratively significant
and mechanically distinct from standard lanyard extraction. The full
design of this moment is reserved for the Montoya Farm mission
development pass.

This principle applies broadly: enemy bodies in full Spetsnaz kit
can be lanyarded to a concealment point; lightly dressed enemies
or civilians must be dragged by hand. The distinction feeds directly
into the body disposal mechanic.

IFAK INTEGRATION

Before or during extraction the player may apply a tourniquet or
basic wound dressing from their IFAK. This is a scripted prompt
interaction — player hits the prompt button and receives a brief
scripted animation. It controls bleeding and preserves casualty
state during the drag. All characters carry an IFAK and are
trained to use it at this basic level.

DEVELOPMENT AGENDA

— Full animation set for lanyard drag (backwards movement,
  weapon handling, terrain variation)
— Hand drag / over-shoulder carry animation set
— Crouching QTE design for specific terrain moments
— Casualty state tracking during drag
— IFAK prompt interaction timing and design
— Enemy body compatibility with lanyard system
— Montoya Farm carry moment full design

20. CASEVAC SYSTEM

STATUS: Confirmed design direction. Partially developed.
Coupled with Casualty Extraction / Retention Lanyard System (19),
Injury System (04), and Allied NPC Squad Mechanics (15).

OVERVIEW

CASEVAC (Casualty Evacuation) is the process of getting a downed
teammate out of the mission area when their wounds exceed what
in-field treatment can manage. It is a mission parameter — not
universally available. Availability is designed per mission and
reflects the operational context, the protagonist's resources,
and the story's progression at that point in the trilogy.

No complex squad management. No routing interfaces. No resource
tracking beyond what the injury system already handles. The player
gets the casualty to a designated safe point and the extraction
is handled from there. Next mission the teammate is back. The
system is designed to feel organic and grounded, not managerial.

MEDIC HIERARCHY — PER TEAM

Every operator carries an IFAK. Basic haemorrhage control —
tourniquet, wound packing — is available to any character at
any time via scripted prompt interaction.

Beyond basic IFAK treatment, each team has a designated medic
with higher treatment capability:

Ducky / AEGIS — Switchblade (LCPL Cassandra Stewart). Confirmed.
Her medic role is mechanically load-bearing. She can stabilize
casualties beyond basic IFAK level. Her presence on a mission
is the primary factor in whether advanced in-field treatment
is available to Ducky's element.

Spook / Victor Two Bravo — Smoke (confirmed). His medic capability
is woven into his character profile, to be developed in the
character development pass. Functions as the analog of Switchblade
for Spook's team.

Romeo / Resistance Cell — Patrick "Pat" Napier (confirmed). Former U.S. Army
68W combat medic, former SPC/E-4, back in the Calloway area as a civilian when
Game 2 opens. Pat is the resistance cell's designated higher-capability medic —
functionally Romeo's analog of Switchblade or Smoke. His medic role is
mechanically load-bearing: he extends treatment beyond basic IFAK level,
stabilizes casualties long enough to move them, and gives the cell a credible
early-war sustainment backbone.

CASEVAC AVAILABILITY — PER CAMPAIGN AND MISSION

CASEVAC availability is a mission design parameter. It must be
determined per mission during the mission outline development pass.
The following are the confirmed availability frameworks per campaign:

DUCKY

Primary CASEVAC asset: PEGASUS (Boeing CH-53 Sea Stallion).
Ducky can call PEGASUS for CASEVAC in missions where the
operational context permits it. Where PEGASUS is unavailable,
allied personnel can receive and extract the casualty if present.
Availability is generally higher for Ducky than the other
protagonists due to DAOG infrastructure.

SPOOK

Most variable availability of the three campaigns. Governed
by CIA infrastructure access and operational cover status.

Covert / stealth missions (e.g. Montoya Farm): NO CASEVAC.
Cover status of the operation prohibits any extraction call.
The Tourists do not officially exist on these missions. The
player manages with IFAK treatment and Smoke's medic capability
only. Getting a casualty out in this context is a significant
design challenge reserved for the mission development pass.

Standard missions with CIA infrastructure access: CASEVAC
available through Agency channels — safe house contacts,
CIA-contracted extraction assets.

Missions involving La Avispa: her Los Sietes contacts may
provide a CASEVAC option in specific missions where she is
present or accessible. This is a mechanical expression of
the growing trust dynamic between Spook and La Avispa.

Further options open as Game 2 and Game 3 story develops
and Spook's network of contacts expands.

ROMEO

Most constrained availability. Reflects his arc from isolated
resistance fighter to organized force leader. CASEVAC availability
is itself a narrative progression mechanic — the player feels
the AFC growing in capability through what resources they
suddenly have access to, without cutscenes announcing it.

Early campaign / resistance cell in occupied Kentucky:
NO CASEVAC or minimal improvised options through the
resistance network. Getting a teammate out is a real
problem with no clean solution.

Mid-campaign as AFC grows: resistance network contacts
begin to provide basic extraction options. Still unreliable,
still improvised.

Late Game 1 / early Game 2: as military elements join
the AFC and Romeo's operational scope expands, more
reliable CASEVAC options become available.

Boston arc (Game 3): British military CASEVAC becomes
available — Royal Marines, SAS, SBS infrastructure around
Lt. Vickers's operation. This is a LATE GAME availability,
not available from the start of the Boston arc. It arrives
when the story says it does — when the coalition has
actually formed around Romeo's operation. The player
feels the British forces' arrival through suddenly having
CASEVAC options they didn't have before. This progression
must be protected during the Game 3 mission design pass.

DLC (Sea Spear / other): CASEVAC availability depends on
the setting of the specific DLC. To be determined during
DLC development.

The physical safe zone to which the player delivers a casualty

21. LEVEL DESIGN PRINCIPLES

STATUS: High-level standing principles. Apply across all campaigns,
missions, and mechanics. Not specific to any single system.
Referenced by: Body Carrying and Disposal (10), Stealth System (12),
and any other mechanic where physical space intersects with gameplay.

OVERVIEW

These principles govern how physical spaces in Project Warriors are
designed and scaled. They are not mechanic-specific — they apply
wherever the geometry, atmosphere, and navigability of a space
affect the player's experience. They should be treated as standing
constraints during mission design, level layout, and art direction
passes across all three campaigns and the DLC.

PRINCIPLE 1 — CLAUSTROPHOBIA AS A DESIGN TOOL,
NOT A DESIGN FAILURE

Tight, oppressive, uncomfortable spaces are valuable for narrative
and atmospheric purposes. Cramped-feeling environments reinforce
tension, danger, and psychological pressure on the player in ways
that open spaces cannot. This is intentional and desirable in the
right contexts — underground complexes, occupied small-town
interiors, covert infiltration environments, ship holds.

The distinction is between:

Intentional claustrophobia — spaces that feel tight through
visual language, lighting, prop density, ceiling height, and
environmental detail, while remaining fully navigable. The player
feels the constraint atmospherically without being mechanically
impeded by it.

Accidental frustration — spaces where the visual promise and
the navigable reality contradict each other. The player sees
what appears to be a passable route and finds they cannot pass.
Or navigation causes repeated collision with environment geometry.
This is a design failure, not a design tool.

The goal is always the first. The second must be avoided.

PRINCIPLE 2 — VISUAL SCALE VS. COLLISION SCALE

The visual and atmospheric impression of a space can and should
be significantly tighter than its actual collision geometry.
A corridor can look barely wide enough to move through while
being built at a scale that causes no navigation problems.
A ceiling can appear oppressively low while clearing character
height comfortably.

Reference: Resident Evil 7 and 8 achieved this consistently —
spaces feel genuinely small and wrong in scale while remaining
fully playable. Silent Hill titles used similar approaches.
This is the target register for Project Warriors environments
that require a cramped or confined feeling.

Practical implication for level designers: design the visual
layer to feel smaller than the collision layer. Use props,
lighting, texture scale, and ceiling detail to compress the
apparent size of a space. Do not compress the actual navigable
geometry to match the visual impression.

PRINCIPLE 3 — NAVIGATION CLARITY

Players must always be able to read where they can and cannot
go. Visual language should communicate passability reliably:

— Spaces that look passable must be passable
— Spaces that look impassable must be impassable
— The boundary between the two must be legible at a glance

Where a space must feel ambiguous for atmospheric reasons —
the player unsure whether a route exists — that ambiguity
should be intentional and resolved quickly. Prolonged
navigational confusion is frustrating, not tense.

PRINCIPLE 4 — BODY DISPOSAL GEOMETRY

Specific to missions requiring the body carrying and disposal
mechanic (section 10): receptacle placement must account for
the approach geometry of the lanyard drag system. The player
moves backwards while dragging. Receptacles must be reachable
with a clear backwards approach angle.

Avoid placing disposal receptacles:
— In dead-end alcoves requiring a turn to enter while dragging
— In spaces too narrow to maneuver with a body in tow
— Behind obstacles that require the player to navigate around
  them while moving backwards

The proximity radius system (see section 10) provides some
forgiveness on final positioning, but approach geometry must
still be considered during level layout.

PRINCIPLE 5 — HUMAN SCALE ENVIRONMENTS

Where appropriate and design-feasible, environments should
be scaled closer to true human scale rather than the slightly
inflated scale common in action games (which exists to prevent
navigation issues and improve combat spacing). Human-scale
environments feel more real, more grounded, and more
claustrophobic where that is the intent.

This is not a universal requirement — combat-heavy open
environments benefit from standard game scale. But interior
spaces in stealth missions, resistance cell safe houses,
underground complexes, and similar environments are strong
candidates for tighter, more human-proportioned geometry
where the team determines it is achievable without creating
navigation problems.

This principle requires evaluation on a per-environment basis
during the level design pass. It is an aspiration with
