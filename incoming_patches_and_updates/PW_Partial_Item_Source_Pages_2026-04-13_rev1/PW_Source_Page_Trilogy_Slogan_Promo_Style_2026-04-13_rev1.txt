01. PURPOSE
================================================================================

This page defines the currently locked campaign-level teaser identity lines and the rules governing promo construction, tone, and music use.

It is not a full marketing bible.
It is the source-of-truth reference for narrative-facing promo identity so future pages stop inheriting stale tagline variants.

================================================================================
03. PROJECT-LEVEL IDENTITY RULES
================================================================================

Do:
- let each campaign feel like its own cultural and tonal lane
- keep the tagline short, declarative, and pressure-bearing
- use music to create productive tension rather than merely mirror mood
- treat teaser identity as a narrative thesis statement, not just hype copy

Do not:
- recycle retired campaign-title language into current-facing materials
- let environmental song picks replace campaign anchor identity
- flatten all three campaigns into the same trailer voice

================================================================================
04. PROMO CONSTRUCTION RULES — LOCKED
================================================================================

Character promo seven-part structure:
1. origin / establishing image
2. first detonation or signature combat ignition
3. character-specific tool / weapon beat
4. deeper action escalation
5. squad / relationship passage
6. final build
7. ending private detail / title card

Recurring technique:
- diegetic sound punctuation
- music partially drops
- real-world sound cuts through
- music returns

Use silence and restraint actively.
Do not score every second as if fear of empty space were a virtue.

================================================================================
05. MUSIC USE RULES — LOCKED
================================================================================

Environmental music rule:
Music used in environments is world texture first, not replacement for campaign anchor identity.

Preferred principle:
productive dissonance > literal mood matching

Meaning:
- warmth against horror can be stronger than grimness against grimness
- quiet sources matter
- low-volume diegetic wrongness often lands harder than obvious dramatic scoring

Drop / caution notes:
- “Run to the Hills” is dropped from active use
- Blonde Redhead “For the Damaged Coda” is dropped completely
- extremely famous tracks should be used sparingly and precisely

Special-use note:
Montoya Farm optional observation-space beat may use Ghost “Square Hammer” as low-volume diegetic wrongness, not overt stylized horror punctuation.

================================================================================
06. CAMPAIGN-SPECIFIC PROMO FEEL
================================================================================

ROMEO
- should feel human, local, occupied, and physically immediate
- warmth, community texture, and civic stakes are load-bearing
- avoid making him only a vengeance vector

DUCKY
- should feel procedural, exact, widening in scale without becoming bombastic
- systems, coalition, and field competence matter
- heroism is carried through steadiness and pressure-control

SPOOK
- should feel hidden, compromised, redacted, and increasingly intimate in its wrongness
- intelligence-world corruption and the hunt for already-buried truth are central
- avoid empty “spy cool” framing

================================================================================
07. HOUSE STYLE FOR TAGLINES
================================================================================

Preferred form:
- all caps acceptable in promo layout
- one sentence or sentence fragment only
- high compression
- no stacked clauses
- declarative over rhetorical unless a deliberate exception has already locked

Good examples from current canon:
- WAR HITS HOME.
- THE SYSTEM FELL. WE DIDN’T.
- YOU CAN’T KILL A DEAD MAN.

Recommended test:
If the line cannot survive alone on a black screen after one cut to silence, it is probably too weak or too verbose.
