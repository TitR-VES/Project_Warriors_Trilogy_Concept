02. ENVIRONMENT THESIS
================================================================================

Bakhmut should not read as generic apocalyptic rubble.
It should read as a real Eastern Ukrainian city ground down by prolonged attritional fighting, civilian abandonment, repeated artillery damage, improvised fortification, winter grime, and surviving structural logic.

The environment language should emphasize:
- cumulative destruction rather than single-event spectacle
- civic fabric under attrition
- layered damage states
- practical military adaptation of ordinary urban space
- mud, dust, soot, concrete powder, torn insulation, exposed rebar, wet interiors, broken glazing, and lived-in military reuse

================================================================================
03. VISUAL RULES
================================================================================

Do:
- preserve the feeling of a real city rather than a set-piece ruin theme park
- show mixed survivability: some structures gutted, some partially usable, some intact enough to matter tactically
- keep street furniture, signage, utility lines, apartment shells, garages, courtyards, tram / road logic, and civilian residue legible
- let fortification feel improvised and repeated over time
- ground the space in Eastern European material culture and built form

Do not:
- beautify destruction
- over-romanticize ruins
- flatten all districts into the same damage density
- turn every street into a cinematic canyon of perfect collapse

================================================================================
04. TACTICAL SPACE LANGUAGE
================================================================================

Favorable recurring geometry:
- broken apartment blocks with partial internal sightlines
- courtyards and service alleys
- garages / workshops / utility rooms
- road choke points created by civilian vehicles, tram infrastructure, concrete obstacles, and collapse debris
- ad hoc trenching or fighting-position logic cutting through urban edges
- layered interiors where one floor is dead, another still tactically usable

Combat-space feel:
- exposure is often vertical as much as frontal
- cover is often dirty, compromised, and temporary
- firing positions should feel discovered / adapted rather than beautifully designed

================================================================================
05. COLOR / WEATHER / MATERIAL REGISTER
================================================================================

Preferred register:
- cold neutrals
- damp concrete
- dirty thaw or ash-grey winter light
- smoke haze
- soot-blackened surfaces
- faded civilian color residues struggling through mud, dust, and impact marks

Use weather to make space heavier, not prettier.

================================================================================
06. PROMPT / ART DIRECTION TEMPLATE
================================================================================

When generating or briefing Bakhmut-facing art, concept, or image-reference work, bias toward:
- real city block logic
- attritional war damage
- Eastern European urban identity
- improvised defense and reuse
- documentary realism over operatic destruction

Compact prompt spine:
"Grounded Eastern Ukrainian urban combat zone shaped by prolonged attritional fighting; real apartment blocks, courtyards, service lanes, damaged civic infrastructure, improvised fortification, mud, soot, rebar, broken glazing, partial interior survivability, winter grime, documentary realism, no stylized apocalypse gloss."
